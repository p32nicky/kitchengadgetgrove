Sourdough Starter Maintenance Calendar for Year‑Round Baking

Keeping a sourdough starter alive is a bit like having a low-maintenance pet. If you feed it and keep it clean, it’ll give you incredible bread for a lifetime. However, the biggest challenge for most home bakers isn't starting the culture—it's maintaining it as the seasons change. Temperature is the primary driver of fermentation, meaning your feeding schedule in July should look very different from your schedule in January.

To help you stay consistent, I’ve put together this sourdough starter maintenance calendar. Whether you bake daily or once a month, adjusting your routine based on the weather will prevent your starter from becoming too acidic or falling dormant.

Spring: Waking Up Your Culture

As the kitchen warms up in March and April, you'll notice your starter peaking faster. If you've been keeping your starter in the fridge over winter, spring is the time to transition back to a regular counter-top feeding schedule if you plan to bake more frequently.

During this transition, focus on consistency. If your kitchen is around 68-72°F, a daily feeding of equal parts flour and water (by weight) is usually sufficient. This is also a great time to refresh your equipment. Summer: Managing High-Speed Fermentation

Summer is when most bakers struggle with "over-fermentation." In a 75-80°F kitchen, the wild yeast and bacteria feast through their food rapidly. If you leave your starter on the counter, it may peak and collapse in just a few hours, leading to a buildup of acetic acid (that sharp, vinegar smell).

To manage this in your sourdough starter maintenance calendar, try these adjustments:

  • Increase Feed Ratios: instead of a 1:1:1 ratio, try 1:2:2 (1 part starter, 2 parts flour, 2 parts water) to give the yeast more food to work through.
  • Use Cool Water: Use filtered water from the fridge rather than lukewarm tap water to slow down the initial fermentation.
  • The Fridge Pivot: If you aren't baking daily, keep your starter in the refrigerator and only bring it out for a "wake up" feed 24 hours before you plan to bake.

Autumn: Slowing Down the Pace

As October and November roll in, the drop in temperature means your starter will take longer to double in size. You might notice that a starter that used to peak in 4 hours now takes 8 to 12 hours. This isn't a sign of weakness; it's just physics.

To keep your starter active during the autumn dip, look for the warmest spot in your kitchen—usually on top of the refrigerator or inside a turned-off oven with the light left on. This is also the season for hearty, rustic loaves. Using the dough scraper from the 3-in-1 Sourdough Whisk Set helps you handle the stickier, higher-hydration doughs common in fall recipes, making it easier to clean your workspace.

Winter: Dormancy and Deep Freezes

Winter is the most challenging time for a sourdough starter maintenance calendar because indoor heating can dry out the air, while drafts near windows can chill your jars. If your kitchen drops below 65°F, your yeast may go dormant.

If you are baking weekly, a daily feeding is still best, but consider using slightly warmer water (about 85°F) to jumpstart the activity. If you are taking a break from baking during the holidays, move your starter to the fridge and feed it once every 7 to 14 days. When you're ready to bake again, give it two or three consecutive room-temperature feedings to ensure it has the strength to lift a heavy loaf of bread.

FAQ

How do I know if my starter is too acidic?

If your starter has a strong smell of nail polish remover (acetone) or a very sharp vinegary scent, it's hungry. This often happens in the summer. Perform two back-to-back feedings every 12 hours to balance the pH levels before using it in a recipe.

Can I use the same feeding schedule all year?

You can, but you'll likely find your timing is off. Because yeast is temperature-dependent, a "set it and forget it" schedule often leads to under-proofed bread in winter and over-proofed bread in summer. Adjusting based on your kitchen's ambient temperature is the secret to consistency.

What is the best way to store starter in the fridge?

Feed your starter until it is active and bubbly, then let it sit for about an hour before popping it in the fridge. This ensures the yeast has plenty of fuel to sustain itself during its dormant period. When you're ready to bake, simply discard and feed as usual.
