Mastering the Base: Perfectly Grilled Corn

Before you can top it, you have to cook it. The foundation of a great corn bar is the corn itself. You want kernels that are tender and have a nice, smoky char. While boiling is easy, grilling imparts that essential flavor profile that screams "backyard BBQ."

Start by peeling back the husks and removing the silk, but keep the husks attached if you plan to use them as a handle. For the best flavor, soak the ears in cold water for about 15 minutes before they hit the grate. This steams the corn inside the husk while the outside gets roasted. Alternatively, shuck the corn completely and brush the ears with olive oil or melted butter. Place them directly on a medium-hot grill, turning them every few minutes. You are looking for golden-brown spots on all sides, which usually takes about 10 to 15 minutes.

The Butter Station: Compound Butters

Butter is non-negotiable, but plain butter is boring. A corn bar shines when you offer a variety of compound butters. These are easy to make a day in advance and store in the fridge until party time. Simply let your butter soften, mix in your ingredients, and roll them into logs using parchment paper.

Here are a few flavor combinations that never fail:

  • Lime & Cilantro: Mix softened butter with fresh lime zest, chopped cilantro, and a pinch of chili powder.
  • Garlic Herb: Combine butter with minced roasted garlic, parsley, chives, and thyme.
  • Spicy Honey: Whisk honey into your butter along with a teaspoon of cayenne pepper or red pepper flakes.
  • Sriracha Mayo: While not a butter, a mix of mayonnaise and Sriracha is the base for Mexican street corn and deserves a spot on the table.

The Toppings Bar

Once the butter is melted, the real fun begins. Set up a station with bowls of dry toppings and seasonings. This allows guests to create their own masterpieces, whether they prefer sweet, spicy, or savory profiles.

For a classic street corn vibe, you need crumbled cotija cheese and Tajín or chili powder. If your crowd likes heat, offer crushed red pepper flakes or pickled jalapeños. For savory notes, put out crispy bacon bits, toasted sesame seeds, or everything bagel seasoning. Fresh elements like chopped scallions, diced red onion, and extra herbs like parsley or basil add a burst of freshness that cuts through the richness of the butter.

Creative Grilled Corn Bar Ideas for a Backyard Party

If you want to switch up the format from the traditional "butter and sit" style, consider these variations to keep things interesting:

The "Elote" Cart: Focus entirely on the Mexican street corn theme. Serve the corn with mayonnaise, lime wedges, cotija cheese, and heavy dustings of chili powder. It is messy but undeniably delicious.

The Mediterranean Corner: Skip the butter and offer a brush of olive oil instead. Top with crumbled feta cheese, dried oregano, lemon zest, and a sprinkle of za'atar.

The Breakfast-for-Dinner Corn: This sounds unusual but works for a late afternoon BBQ. Offer a cinnamon-sugar butter mix and toppings like chopped strawberries, pecans, or a drizzle of maple syrup.

FAQ

How do I keep grilled corn warm for a party?

Grilled corn cools down surprisingly fast. To keep it warm while guests are filling their plates, you can place the cooked ears in a cooler lined with clean towels or newspaper; the insulation will keep them hot for over an hour. Alternatively, keep them on a top rack of the grill set to low heat or in a slow cooker set to "warm."

Can I prep the corn in advance?

Absolutely. You can shuck and clean the corn a day before and store it in a sealed bag in the fridge. The compound butters can also be made days ahead and frozen, then pulled out to soften before serving. You can also chop all your dry toppings like cheese, herbs, and bacon the night before.

What is the best way to remove silk from corn?

The most reliable method is to use a soft vegetable brush under running water after shucking. For stubborn silk, try rubbing the ear with a damp paper towel. If you are grilling with the husks on, peeling them back partially, removing the silk, and then re-wrapping the husk helps keep the corn clean while it grills.
