If you’ve ever tried to mimic the airy, slightly sour bite of a traditional sourdough loaf in a pizza crust, you know the challenge: a thin, crisp base that still has that signature tang. This sourdough pizza crust recipe walks you through the entire process—from feeding your starter to shaping and baking—so you can serve up restaurant‑quality pies at home.

Why Use Sourdough for Pizza?

Sourdough brings three key benefits to pizza dough. First, the natural fermentation develops flavor that commercial yeast can’t match. Second, the acidity strengthens gluten, giving the crust a better rise and a chewier interior. Third, the slower rise reduces the amount of dough you need to handle, making the final stretch easier. When you combine those qualities with a hot stone or steel, you get a crust that’s crispy on the bottom, airy on the inside, and just a touch tangy on the palate.

  • 100 g active sourdough starter (100% hydration)
  • 350 g bread flour (or a blend of 70% bread, 30% whole‑wheat)
  • 200 g water, lukewarm
  • 10 g sea salt
  • 1 Tbsp olive oil (optional, for a softer crumb)
  • 1‑2 Tbsp cornmeal or semolina for dusting

Equipment: digital scale, mixing bowl, kitchen towel, and a pizza stone or steel preheated to 500°F (260°C). The Sourdough Whisk Set will make the initial mixing and turning of the dough a breeze.

Day‑One: Autolyse and Mix

Start by combining the flour and water in a large bowl. Use the Danish dough whisk from the Sourdough Whisk Set to blend until no dry spots remain. Let the mixture sit for 30 minutes; this autolyse step hydrates the flour, making the later kneading gentler.

After the autolyse, add the active starter, salt, and olive oil. Switch to the dough whisk again, then use the scraper to fold the dough in the bowl for about 2 minutes. You’re looking for a shaggy, slightly sticky mass that starts to pull away from the sides.

Bulk Fermentation & Folding

Cover the bowl with a damp towel and let the dough rise at room temperature (around 70°F/21°C) for 4‑5 hours. Every 30 minutes during the first 2 hours, perform a set of stretch‑and‑folds: wet your hands, grab a portion of the dough, stretch it upward, and fold it over the rest. This technique builds gluten without intensive kneading and works especially well with sourdough’s naturally weaker gluten network.

By the end of bulk fermentation, the dough should have risen roughly 30‑40 % and feel smoother and more elastic. If it’s still very sticky, let it rest a few more minutes before moving on.

Shaping the Pizza Base

Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Divide it into two equal pieces for 12‑inch pizzas, or keep it whole for a larger, rustic pie. Using the dough scraper, gently round each piece into a tight ball and let them rest, covered, for 20‑30 minutes. This relaxation period makes the final stretch much easier.

When you’re ready, press the dough from the center outward, leaving a slightly thicker rim. If you prefer a super‑thin crust, you can transfer the dough onto a piece of parchment paper and use your hands to stretch it to the desired diameter. The dough should be thin enough to see the perforations but still retain a bit of spring.

Baking the Perfect Crust

Slide the parchment‑lined pizza onto a preheated stone or steel. Bake at 500°F (260°C) for 6‑8 minutes, or until the edges are golden and the bottom is crisp. If you’re adding toppings, do so after the first 2‑3 minutes of baking to avoid soggy crust.

While the pizza finishes, you can use the lame from the 3‑in‑1 Sourdough Whisk Set to score a focaccia or a rustic loaf with the same starter. The tool’s sharp blade gives clean cuts without tearing the dough, a nice bonus for any sourdough enthusiast.

Tips for Consistent Results

• Starter strength matters: Your starter should be bubbly and pass the “float test” before you begin.

• Temperature control: If your kitchen is cooler, extend the bulk fermentation by an hour or two. Warmer kitchens may need a shorter rise.

• Don’t over‑hydrate: The dough should feel tacky but manageable. Adjust water by a few grams if needed.

• Use the whisk set: The Danish dough whisk mixes without tearing gluten, and the scraper makes clean transfers from bowl to surface—small tools that save big time.

FAQ

Can I freeze the sourdough pizza crust dough?

Yes. After the first rise, shape the dough into balls, wrap each tightly in plastic, and freeze. Thaw overnight in the fridge, then let sit at room temperature for 30 minutes before stretching.

Do I need to pre‑bake the crust before adding toppings?

For very wet toppings (e.g., fresh mozzarella), a 2‑minute blind bake helps prevent sogginess. For most sauces and cheeses, you can add them after the crust has been in the oven for a couple of minutes.
