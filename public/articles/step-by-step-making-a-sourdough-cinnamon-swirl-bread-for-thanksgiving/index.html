  • 500 g bread flour (about 4 cups)
  • 350 g water (1½ cups), lukewarm
  • 100 g active sourdough starter (1/2 cup), fed 4‑6 hours before
  • 10 g salt (1 ½ tsp)
  • 75 g brown sugar (½ cup)
  • 60 g butter, melted
  • 2 tsp ground cinnamon
  • Optional: ¼ tsp nutmeg for extra warmth

For tools, you’ll need a large mixing bowl, a kitchen scale, a bench scraper (the dough scraper from the Sourdough Whisk Set works perfectly), a Danish dough whisk for incorporating the starter, and a bread scoring lame for the final slash.

Step 1: Autolyse the Dough

Combine the flour and water in a bowl. Using the Danish dough whisk, mix until no dry spots remain—no need to knead yet. Cover the bowl with a damp towel and let it sit for 30 minutes. This autolyse period hydrates the flour, improves gluten development, and yields a more open crumb—ideal for a swirl bread.

Step 2: Incorporate Starter, Sugar, and Butter

After the autolyse, add the active sourdough starter, melted butter, and brown sugar. Stir with the dough whisk until fully integrated. Sprinkle the salt over the surface and fold it in using the dough scraper. The dough will feel sticky; that’s normal for a high‑hydration loaf.

Step 3: Bulk Fermentation and Folding

Let the dough rise at room temperature (around 75°F) for about 4 hours. Every 30 minutes during the first 2 hours, perform a set of stretch‑and‑folds: grab a portion of the dough, stretch it upward, and fold it over the rest. Use the scraper to help lift the sticky mass without tearing it. These folds build strength without intensive kneading.

Step 4: Prepare the Cinnamon Swirl

While the dough rests, mix the cinnamon, nutmeg (if using), and an extra tablespoon of brown sugar in a small bowl. Once the bulk fermentation is complete, turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Using the dough scraper, flatten it into a rectangle about ½‑inch thick.

Brush the surface lightly with melted butter, then sprinkle the cinnamon‑sugar mixture evenly. Starting from the short side, roll the dough tightly into a log. Pinch the seam closed and set the log aside to rest for 15 minutes.

Step 5: Shape, Proof, and Score

After the short rest, gently flatten the log again, then roll it up once more to reinforce the spiral. Transfer the shaped loaf into a well‑floured banneton or a parchment‑lined proofing basket. Cover and let it proof for 2‑3 hours, or overnight in the refrigerator for a deeper flavor.

When the dough has risen about 30 % and the surface feels slightly domed, preheat your oven to 450°F (230°C) with a Dutch oven inside. Once hot, carefully invert the loaf onto a parchment‑covered baking stone, and use the scoring lame from the Sourdware Whisk Set to make a swift slash about ½‑inch deep across the top. This allows steam to escape and creates a beautiful crust.

Bake to Perfection

Place the loaf in the preheated Dutch oven, cover, and bake for 20 minutes. Then remove the lid and bake an additional 20‑25 minutes, or until the crust is deep golden brown and the internal temperature reaches 200°F (93°C). Let the bread cool on a wire rack for at least 30 minutes before slicing; this rest lets the crumb set and the cinnamon swirl settle.

Serving Suggestions for Thanksgiving

Slice the sourdough cinnamon swirl bread thanksgiving‑style and serve warm with a dollop of whipped butter or a drizzle of maple syrup. It pairs beautifully with roasted turkey, creamy mashed potatoes, and cranberry sauce. Leftovers make excellent French toast the next day—just dip thick slices in an egg‑milk mixture, fry, and top with powdered sugar.

FAQ

Can I use a different flour?

Yes. Whole‑wheat or spelt flour can replace up to 30 % of the bread flour for a nuttier flavor, but adjust hydration slightly as these flours absorb more liquid.

Do I need a Dutch oven?

A Dutch oven creates steam that yields a crisp crust. If you don’t have one, place a metal pan on the lower rack and add boiling water to generate steam.

How long can I keep the bread?

Store the loaf wrapped in a clean kitchen towel at room temperature for up to three days. For longer storage, slice and freeze; toast directly from frozen for best results.
