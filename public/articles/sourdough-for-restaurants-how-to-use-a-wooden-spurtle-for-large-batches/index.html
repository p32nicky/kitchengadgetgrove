Optimizing Your Mixing Workflow

To get the most out of your tools when handling large volumes, try this sequence to ensure a smooth, bubble-filled starter every time:

  • The Initial Incorporate: Use the Danish-style dough stick from the set to break up the initial dry flour pockets. The loops allow air to enter the mix quickly.
  • The Deep Stir: Switch to the skinny wooden spurtle to reach the corners and the bottom. Use a circular motion, then a vertical "lifting" motion to pull the heavier flour from the base.
  • The Wall Scrape: Use the flat edge of the spurtle to scrape down the sides of the jar or bucket. This prevents dried-on starter crusts that can harbor unwanted bacteria.
  • The Final Fold: Use the spurtle to fold the mixture over itself, ensuring the temperature is consistent throughout the batch.

Maintaining Your Wooden Tools in a Commercial Kitchen

In a high-volume restaurant, your tools take a beating. To keep your wooden spurtle and dough whisk in top shape, avoid the industrial dishwasher. The high heat and harsh chemicals will crack the wood and strip the natural oils, leading to splinters in your dough.

Instead, implement a quick hand-wash station. Rinse the tools immediately after use with hot soapy water. Organization is key when managing large batches. We recommend assigning a specific spurtle to each stage of your process. One for the initial starter feed and another for the final mix. This prevents cross-contamination and ensures that your tools are always where your staff expects them to be.

FAQ

Can a wooden spurtle replace a commercial stand mixer?

For the initial mixing of the starter or the early stages of a dough mix, yes. However, for the bulk fermentation and final kneading of massive batches, you'll still want a spiral mixer. The spurtle is meant for the "prep and stir" phase, not the heavy kneading phase.

Why use wood instead of silicone or stainless steel?

Wood is naturally non-reactive to the acidity of sourdough. It also provides a specific type of tension and "grip" on the dough that slippery silicone lacks, making it easier to feel if there are still dry clumps of flour in the mix.

How often should I replace my wooden stirring tools?

In a restaurant environment, replace them when you notice deep cracks or significant fraying. With proper oiling and hand-washing, a high-quality wooden spurtle should last several months of heavy daily use.
