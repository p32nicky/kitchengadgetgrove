Essential gear for outdoor sourdough camping

Before you even think about stirring, make sure you have the basics:

  1. Mason jars (or glass containers) – 1‑quart jars are perfect for a starter and easy to carry.
  2. Organic flour – Whole‑wheat or rye gives the most flavor; keep a small bag in your dry sack.
  3. Water – Use filtered or boiled water to avoid wild microbes that can overwhelm a starter.
  4. Wooden Spurtle & Sourdough Starter Stirrer Set (2 PCS) – One stick for mixing, the other for scraping the jar clean.
  5. Portable heat source – A lightweight camp stove or a cast‑iron Dutch oven over coals works well.

With these items in your pack, you’re ready to feed, stir, and bake your sourdough wherever the trail leads.

Feeding your starter on the trail

Maintaining a starter in the backcountry follows the same math as at home: 1 part starter, 1 part flour, 1 part water by weight. Because a kitchen scale is rarely an option on a hike, use volume approximations. One cup of starter (about 240 ml) mixes with one cup of flour and one cup of water. The wooden spurtle’s slim shape lets you fold the flour into the liquid without splashing, and its wooden tip can gently press any stubborn clumps against the jar wall.

After mixing, cover the jar with a breathable cloth or a loose‑fit lid. The spurtle’s smooth surface won’t leave scratches, so you can reuse the same jar for weeks without weakening the glass.

Baking sourdough in a camp kitchen

When it’s time to bake, you have two reliable methods: a Dutch oven on coals or a cast‑iron skillet with a lid. Both benefit from the wooden spurtle’s ability to shape a dough without tearing it.

  • Pre‑heat your Dutch oven by placing it on hot coals for 10–15 minutes. This creates an even oven‑like environment.
  • Transfer the dough onto a parchment circle. Use the spurtle to gently lift the dough from the jar and place it on the parchment without crushing the bubbles.
  • Score the top with the tip of the spurtle. The tapered point makes clean, shallow cuts that let steam escape, giving you a crisp crust.
  • Cover and bake for 20 minutes, then remove the lid and bake another 10–15 minutes for a golden finish.

If you’re using a skillet, the same scoring technique applies, and you can finish the loaf under a campfire’s radiant heat for that smoky flavor.

Cleaning the spurtle in the wilderness

One of the greatest advantages of the wooden spurtle set is its low‑maintenance clean‑up. After stirring, simply scrape any remaining dough into the jar with the second spurtle stick. Rinse the sticks in a small bowl of water, wipe them with a cloth, and let them air‑dry. Because the wood is naturally antimicrobial, you’ll rarely need soap—just a quick rinse is enough to keep flavors from transferring between batches.

If you’re camping in an area with limited water, give the spurtle a quick wipe with a damp napkin and store it in a dry pocket until you return to a base camp. The wood’s natural oils protect it from cracking, so a few weeks of occasional use won’t degrade the tool.

Tips for making the most of your outdoor sourdough experience

• Keep the starter warm during the day by tucking the jar into a fleece‑lined cooler. The wooden spurtle’s insulated handle won’t conduct heat, so the starter stays at the ideal 75‑80 °F.

• Use a thin layer of flour on the spurtle before each stir. This reduces sticking and makes cleanup even easier.

• Rotate the spurtle between mixing and scraping to avoid cross‑contamination if you’re feeding multiple starters.

• Pack the spurtles in a small cloth bag to protect the wood from drops and to keep them separate from metal utensils.

How do I prevent the wooden spurtle from warping in humid conditions?
