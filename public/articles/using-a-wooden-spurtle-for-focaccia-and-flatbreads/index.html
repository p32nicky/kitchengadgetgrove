Step‑by‑Step: Mixing Your Focaccia Dough with a Spurtle

  1. Combine the basics: In a large bowl, mix flour, salt, yeast, and water until a shaggy mass forms.
  2. Fold with the spurtle: Use the skinny wooden spurtle to lift and fold the dough, incorporating the flour into the liquid. This gentle motion builds gluten without over‑kneading.
  3. Add olive oil and herbs: Sprinkle olive oil, rosemary, or thyme. Fold again with the spurtle to evenly distribute the aromatics.
  4. First rise: Cover and let rise until doubled. Use the spurtle to gently press out any large bubbles.
  5. Shape and second rise: Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Shape into a rectangle or round, then use the spurtle to create a shallow rim and to press down the dough evenly.
  6. Bake: Drizzle more olive oil, sprinkle sea salt, and bake at 425°F (220°C) for 20–25 minutes.

Using the Spurtle to Scrape and Store Sourdough Starter

The same spurtle that handles focaccia dough also excels at mixing sourdough starter. Its thin blade slides easily into the starter, whisking it without splattering. When feeding, the Danish‑style stick is perfect for scraping the jar walls, ensuring every bit of starter is incorporated. This reduces waste and keeps your starter active.

Tips for Maintaining Your Wooden Spurtle

  • After each use, wipe the spurtle with a damp cloth and dry immediately to prevent cracking.
  • A light coat of food‑safe mineral oil every few months keeps the wood supple.
  • Never soak the spurtle in hot water; it can warp or split.

FAQ

Can I use a wooden spurtle on a non‑stick pan?

Yes, a wooden spurtle is safe on non‑stick surfaces. Its gentle scraping action won’t scratch the coating.

How do I clean a wooden spurtle after baking?

Simply wipe it with a damp cloth, rinse if needed, and dry immediately. For deep stains, a mild soap solution works, but avoid soaking.

Will a wooden spurtle affect the flavor of my dough?

A light, natural wood flavor can subtly enhance your dough, especially with olive oil or herbs. Most bakers find it a pleasant addition rather than a distraction.
