Why Use a Wooden Spurtle for Holiday Bread?

For those unfamiliar, a spurtle is a traditional Scottish tool. While it looks like a simple stick, its design is intentional. Unlike a spoon, which pushes dough around, a spurtle cuts through the mixture, making it ideal for stirring thick batters and heavy doughs without overworking the gluten.

When you're prepping a dense Christmas bread—perhaps one loaded with dried currants, orange peel, and cinnamon—you want a tool that can fold in those heavy inclusions without deflating the air your yeast has worked so hard to create. The skinny profile of the spurtle allows you to stir and scrape simultaneously, ensuring no pockets of dry flour remain at the bottom of your mixing bowl.

Top Tips for Holiday Baking Wooden Spurtle Christmas Bread

To get the most out of your tools this season, try these practical tips to ensure your loaves come out light, airy, and professional:

  • Deglaze Your Jar: Use your skinny spurtle to scrape every bit of starter from the sides of the jar. This prevents mold growth on the glass and ensures an accurate feeding ratio.
  • The "Folding" Technique: Instead of stirring in circles, use the spurtle to lift the dough from the bottom and fold it over the top. This strengthens the gluten structure of your Christmas bread.
  • Avoid Over-mixing: Use the Danish whisk for the first 2-3 minutes of mixing, then switch to the spurtle for folding in nuts, fruits, or chocolate chips.
  • Protect Your Bowls: Since these tools are wood, they are non-scratch. You can use them in your favorite ceramic or non-stick bowls without worrying about permanent scuff marks.

Maintaining Your Wooden Baking Tools

To ensure your spurtle and dough whisk last through many holiday seasons, avoid the dishwasher. The high heat and harsh detergents can cause the wood to crack or warp.

Instead, hand-wash them with warm, soapy water immediately after use. For sourdough tools, a quick rinse is usually enough, but once a month, rub them with a food-grade mineral oil. This creates a barrier against moisture and prevents the wood from absorbing odors from your spices or starters.

FAQ

Can I use a wooden spurtle for things other than bread?

Absolutely. The skinny spurtle is excellent for stirring matcha, mixing small batches of salad dressing in narrow jars, or scraping the last bit of jam out of a preserve jar.

Is a Danish whisk better than a stand mixer?

For many recipes, yes. While a stand mixer is powerful, a Danish whisk gives you more "feel" for the dough. It allows you to sense exactly when the dough has reached the right consistency before you move to the kneading stage.

Why is wood better than silicone for stirring sourdough?

Wood is naturally durable and provides a certain rigidity that silicone often lacks. When dealing with thick, sticky sourdough starter, the firmness of a wooden spurtle allows you to stir through the resistance without the tool bending.
