Corn is a camping staple, but flipping hot cobs with tongs can be clumsy, and eating them with your bare hands often leads to messy fingers and burnt fingertips. For the best results, I recommend the "foil-pack" method for corn. Shuck the corn, rub each ear with a mixture of softened butter, smoked paprika, and garlic powder, then wrap them tightly in aluminum foil. Grill them for about 15-20 minutes, turning occasionally. Once the kernels are tender, unwrap them and put them directly on the grate for 2-3 minutes to get those beautiful blackened char marks. Hearty Camping Grilled Corn and Veggie Recipes

While corn is the star, adding a variety of hearty vegetables creates a complete meal. The key is to choose vegetables that cook at a similar rate or separate them by their cooking times. Here are three foolproof combinations:

  • The Summer Medley: Zucchini rounds, red bell pepper chunks, and red onion wedges. Toss them in olive oil, dried oregano, salt, and pepper. Grill in a veggie basket or on skewers until the edges are caramelized.
  • The Root Roast: Thick-cut carrots and baby potatoes. Since these take longer, parboil them at home first. At the campsite, toss them with rosemary and olive oil and grill until the skins are crispy.
  • The Spicy Street Mix: Grilled corn and poblano peppers. Once charred, slather the corn in crema, sprinkle with cotija cheese and lime juice, and serve alongside the blistered poblanos.

Pro Tips for Grilling Veggies in the Wild

Grilling outdoors presents a few challenges that you don't face in a backyard kitchen. First, wind can fluctuate your heat. If you're using charcoal, create a "two-zone" fire: a hot side for searing and a cooler side for vegetables that need more time to soften without burning.

Second, prep your marinade at home. Fill a reusable silicone bag or a mason jar with your oil, acids (like lemon or lime), and spices. This saves you from bringing five different spice jars to the woods and ensures your vegetables are evenly seasoned. When you're ready to cook, simply dump the pre-cut veggies into the marinade and let them sit for 10 minutes while the grill heats up.

FAQ

Should I leave the husks on when grilling corn?

It depends on the result you want. Grilling in the husk steams the corn, keeping it juicy and sweet, but you won't get any char. For a smoky, grilled flavor, shuck the corn first or remove the husks after steaming and finish them directly on the grate.

How do I prevent small vegetables from falling through the grill grates?

The best way is to use a grill basket or a perforated grill mat. If you don't have those, thread your vegetables onto skewers. Just make sure to soak wooden skewers in water for 30 minutes first so they don't catch fire over the coals.

What is the best oil for high-heat camping grills?

Avoid extra virgin olive oil for high-heat searing, as it has a lower smoke point and can taste bitter when burnt. Instead, use avocado oil or grapeseed oil. They can handle the intense heat of a campfire without breaking down.
