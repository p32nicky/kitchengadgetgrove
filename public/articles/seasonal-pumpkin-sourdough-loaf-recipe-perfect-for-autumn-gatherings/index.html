Gather Your Ingredients and Tools

Before you start, measure everything out. Sourdough is forgiving, but precision helps when you’re aiming for a loaf that looks as good as it tastes.

  • 150 g active sourdough starter (100 % hydration)
  • 350 g bread flour
  • 100 g whole‑wheat flour
  • 200 g canned pumpkin puree (unsweetened)
  • 250 ml warm water
  • 30 g honey or maple syrup
  • 10 g salt
  • 1 ½ tsp ground cinnamon
  • ½ tsp ground nutmeg
  • ¼ tsp ground clove

For equipment, the 3‑in‑1 Sourdough Whisk Set is a game‑changer. The Danish dough whisk blends the starter and wet ingredients without over‑mixing, the dough scraper helps you keep the work surface clean, and the bread scoring lame gives you professional‑looking cuts on the final loaf.

Step‑by‑Step: From Autolyse to Bake

1. Autolyse (30 min)
Mix the bread flour, whole‑wheat flour, and warm water in a large bowl until just combined. Cover and let rest. This hydrates the flour, developing gluten without kneading.

2. Incorporate the Starter and Pumpkin
Using the Danish dough whisk from the 3‑in‑1 Sourdough Whisk Set, whisk the active starter, pumpkin puree, and honey into the autolysed dough. Whisk until the mixture is uniform; you’ll notice a slightly sticky texture.

3. Bulk Fermentation (4‑5 hrs)
Every 30 minutes, perform a set of stretch‑and‑folds. The dough scraper makes this step easy—grab a portion of dough, lift, and fold it over itself. After the final fold, let the dough rest undisturbed.

4. Add the Spices and Salt
After the first hour of bulk fermentation, sprinkle the cinnamon, nutmeg, clove, and salt over the dough. Use the whisk briefly to distribute the spices evenly without tearing the gluten network.

5. Shaping
Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Using the dough scraper, gently shape it into a tight boule or oval, depending on your pan. Place the shaped loaf into a well‑floured banneton or a parchment‑lined loaf pan.

6. Proof (overnight)
Cover the dough and refrigerate overnight (12‑14 hrs). This slow proof deepens flavor and makes scoring easier.

7. Scoring and Baking
Preheat your oven to 475°F with a Dutch oven inside. When hot, carefully transfer the loaf onto parchment, then use the bread scoring lame from the 3‑in‑1 Sourdough Whisk Set to make 2‑3 shallow cuts on the surface. This directs the expansion and creates that classic sourdough look.

Place the loaf inside the Dutch oven, cover, and bake 20 minutes. Remove the lid, lower the temperature to 425°F, and bake another 20‑25 minutes until the crust is deep golden and the internal temperature reaches 200°F.

Tips for Success (and Common Mistakes to Avoid)

• Starter Strength: A vigorous starter gives the best rise. Feed it 4‑6 hours before mixing and ensure it’s bubbly.

• Hydration Balance: Pumpkin adds moisture, so watch the dough’s consistency. If it feels too wet, add a tablespoon of flour during the stretch‑and‑folds.

• Scoring Confidence: The lame’s razor‑sharp blade cuts cleanly. Practice on a small piece of dough first if you’re new to scoring.

• Cooling: Let the loaf cool on a wire rack for at least an hour before slicing. This finishes the crumb set and prevents a gummy interior.

Serving Ideas and Storage

Slice the warm pumpkin sourdough loaf and toast it with a smear of butter, a drizzle of maple syrup, or a dollop of cream cheese and toasted pecans for a festive appetizer. Leftovers keep well wrapped at room temperature for up to three days, or frozen for longer storage. Reheat slices in a toaster oven to restore that fresh‑baked crunch.

FAQ

Can I use fresh pumpkin instead of canned puree?

Yes. Roast or steam fresh pumpkin, then puree it until smooth. Expect a slightly thinner consistency, so you may need to reduce the water by 20‑30 ml.

Do I need a Dutch oven, or can I bake on a stone?

A Dutch oven creates steam that gives the crust its signature crunch. If you prefer a stone, place a pan of hot water on the lower rack to generate steam, and bake with a covered bowl or a silicone oven‑safe lid.

Is the 3‑in‑1 Sourdough Whisk Set suitable for gluten‑free doughs?

The whisk and scraper work well with any dough consistency, including gluten‑free mixes. The lame can still score the surface, though gluten‑free loaves may not spring as dramatically.
