Common Starter Issues and How the Spurtle Fixes Them

Below are the most frequent problems bakers encounter and the specific ways a wooden spurtle helps.

  1. Hollow pockets of inactive starter. When you stir with a spoon, the thick liquid can glide over the surface, leaving pockets that don’t get fed. The spurtle’s narrow profile penetrates every corner, ensuring uniform distribution of flour and water.
  2. Scraped‑off crust (hooch) clinging to the jar. Hooch is a sign of dehydration, but it’s also hard to remove with a spoon. The wooden spurtle’s smooth edge slides under the crust, lifting it cleanly without leaving residue.
  3. Over‑mixing that stresses the yeast. A vigorous stir can break the gluten network and stress the wild yeast. The spurtle’s gentle motion mixes just enough to incorporate feed without over‑working the culture.
  4. Glass jar scratches that harbor mold. Repeated use of metal tools creates micro‑scratches. The non‑scratch surface of the wooden spurtle keeps the jar pristine, reducing contamination risk.

Step‑by‑Step: Using the Wooden Spurtle for a Healthy Starter

Follow this routine each feeding to maximize the benefits of your spurtle.

  • 1. Gather your tools. Pull out the 2 PCS Wooden Spurtle Spatula set, a clean glass jar, and your preferred flour and water.
  • 2. Discard or reserve. Remove the amount of starter you’ll keep (usually 50 g) and set it aside. The remaining starter will be refreshed.
  • 3. Add feed. Pour the measured flour and water into the jar. The ratio is usually 1:1:1 (starter:flour:water) by weight.
  • 4. Stir with the spurtle. Insert the skinny wooden spurtle, and use a gentle, clockwise motion to fold the flour into the liquid. The thin tip reaches the bottom, while the broader edge scrapes the sides, eliminating any dry clumps.
  • 5. Scrape the jar. After mixing, run the spurtle along the interior wall to lift any stuck bits. This step prevents crust formation and keeps the starter aerated.
  • 6. Cover and rest. Replace the lid loosely to allow gases to escape. The starter will show bubbles within 6–12 hours if the spurtle has mixed it evenly.

Repeat this routine daily, and you’ll notice a more consistent rise, fewer off‑flavors, and a smoother texture.

Maintaining Your Wooden Spurtle

Wooden tools last for years with proper care. Rinse the spurtle with hot water after each use—no soap is needed, as it can soak into the wood. Let it air‑dry completely before storing it in a dry drawer or hanging it on a utensil rack. If the wood starts to look dried out, rub a light coat of food‑grade mineral oil into the surface; this restores its natural moisture barrier.
