Why a Wooden Spurtle is Essential for Sourdough

For those unfamiliar, a spurtle is a traditional Scottish stirring tool. Unlike a spoon, which pushes ingredients around, the slender, rounded profile of a spurtle cuts through thick starters and doughs. This is critical when your starter reaches that peak thickness where it becomes a sticky paste.

The Wooden Spurtle & Sourdough Starter Stirrer Set (2 PCS) solves the common "dead zone" problem in jars. Because the spurtle is skinny and tapered, it can reach the absolute bottom and the curved edges of your container. This ensures every bit of old starter is integrated with the new flour and water, preventing pockets of unmixed flour that can lead to inconsistent fermentation.

Maintaining Your Wooden Baking Tools

Wood is the preferred material for sourdough tools because it doesn't react with the acidity of the starter. However, wood requires a bit of care to last for years. Never put your spurtle or dough whisk in the dishwasher; the high heat and harsh detergents will cause the wood to crack and warp.

Instead, hand-wash your tools with warm, soapy water immediately after use. If you notice the wood looking dry or feeling rough, rub a small amount of food-grade mineral oil into the grain. This creates a moisture barrier that prevents the wood from absorbing odors and makes it even easier to scrape sticky dough off the surface.

FAQ

Why use wood instead of silicone or metal?

Metal can sometimes react with the acidic nature of a long-fermented sourdough starter, and silicone often lacks the rigidity needed to stir very thick, high-hydration doughs. Wood is naturally antimicrobial, durable, and provides the perfect balance of flexibility and strength.

Can I use a spurtle for other things in the kitchen?

Absolutely. While they are amazing for sourdough, spurtles are excellent for stirring risotto, mixing salad dressings in narrow jars, or stirring thick stews without splashing.

How often should I oil my wooden dough tools?

Generally, once every few months or whenever the wood starts to look "ashy." A quick coat of mineral oil keeps the wood sealed and prevents the sourdough from sticking to the stirrer.
