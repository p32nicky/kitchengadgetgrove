What Is Overproofing and Why It Happens

Overproofing is when the dough ferments too long, causing the gluten network to break down and the crumb to collapse. You’ll notice a sour, overly airy loaf that spreads out of the pan or even tears. The culprit is usually a temperature rise or a misjudged fermentation schedule. In a few minutes you can turn a disaster into a learning experience.

How to Rescue an Overproofed Loaf

1. Cold Shock: Place the dough in the refrigerator for 2–4 hours. The cold stops fermentation and tightens the gluten. 2. Re‑knead Lightly: Give the dough a gentle fold to rebuild structure without overworking it. 3. Re‑proof Briefly: Let it sit at room temperature for 30–45 minutes until it shows a modest rise. 4. Preventing Overproofing in the First Place

Timing is everything. Here’s a quick checklist:

  1. Know your starter’s activity level. A mature starter ferments faster.
  2. Keep the dough at a consistent 75–78°F (24–26°C). Even a 5°F swing can double the rise time.
  3. Use a thermometer to monitor bulk fermentation. When the dough has expanded by 50–70% and has a few bubbles, it’s ready.
  4. Don’t skip the bulk fermentation. Skipping it leads to a rushed proof and higher risk of overproofing.

Quick Fixes for Future Proofs

Keep a proofing log: note the time, temperature, and volume change. If you notice a pattern of overproofing, adjust the fermentation time by 15–30 minutes. Also, consider a cold retard in the fridge overnight; this slows down yeast activity and gives you a more forgiving proofing window.

FAQ

How long should I let my sourdough dough rise before it’s overproofed?

It varies, but a good rule of thumb is to stop bulk fermentation once the dough has increased in volume by about 50–70% and has visible bubbles. Overproofing often occurs when the dough triples in size or more.

Can I bake a loaf that’s already overproofed?

Yes, but the crumb may be gummy or collapse. Use the cold shock method to tighten gluten, shape carefully, and score with a lame. The finish will be softer, but it’s still edible and can be a great learning tool.
