While most of us don't have a professional rotisserie pit in the backyard, you can mimic that vertical roasting effect indoors. Choosing Your Cuts for the Vertical Skewer

Not every cut of beef works for vertical roasting. For authentic Brazilian flavors, you want cuts with a significant fat cap. Picanha (sirloin cap) is the gold standard here. The thick layer of fat on top protects the meat and provides a rich flavor profile as it melts during the roasting process.

If you can't find Picanha, a thick-cut top sirloin or a center-cut pork shoulder also works well. The key is to cut the meat into large, uniform blocks or thick steaks. This ensures that when you stack them on the Vertical Gyro Pan with Skewer for Oven, the heat penetrates evenly through the center of the stack.

Preparing the Meat for Brazilian Churrasco in Gyro Pan

Brazilian steakhouse meats are famous for their simplicity. Over-marinating can mask the quality of the beef. For a true churrasco experience, stick to coarse sea salt (sal grosso). Rub the meat generously with salt about 30 minutes before cooking.

For those who prefer more aromatics, a garlic-herb rub is a great alternative. Mix minced garlic, cracked black pepper, and a touch of olive oil. Apply this to the lean side of the meat, but keep the fat cap clean—you want that fat to render naturally without burning any garlic bits on the surface.

Assembling and Roasting Your Vertical Roast

Loading your Vertical Gyro Pan with Skewer for Oven requires a bit of strategy to ensure stability. Start by sliding your largest, heaviest piece of meat to the bottom of the skewer. Stack subsequent pieces tightly; the tighter the stack, the more consistent the cooking temperature across the meat.

Follow these steps for the best results:

  • Preheat your oven to 400°F (200°C) for a strong initial sear.
  • Secure the meat firmly on the stainless steel skewer to prevent slipping.
  • Place the skewer into the pan, ensuring it is centered to catch all the drippings.
  • Roast for 45 to 75 minutes, depending on the thickness of your meat stack.
  • Use a meat thermometer to pull the roast when the internal temperature reaches 130°F for medium-rare.

The Art of the Vertical Slice

The magic of the vertical roast happens during the carving process. Instead of slicing the meat into traditional steaks, use a sharp carving knife to shave thin, succulent strips from the outer edge of the roast. This is exactly how shawarma and al pastor are served, and it works perfectly for churrasco.

As you shave off the outer layers, the inner meat continues to cook slowly in the oven. This means you can serve a continuous stream of perfectly cooked meat to your guests throughout the meal. Collect the shavings on a platter and drizzle a bit of the rendered fat from the bottom of the pan back over the meat for extra richness.

Essential Sides for Your Home Churrascaria

To complete the meal, you need the traditional accompaniments that cut through the richness of the beef. A homemade Chimichurri sauce (parsley, garlic, oregano, oil, and vinegar) is non-negotiable. The acidity of the vinegar balances the heavy fat of the Picanha.

Pair your meat with a side of vinaigrette (vinagrete)—a Brazilian salad of finely diced tomatoes, onions, and bell peppers in vinegar. For a starch, roasted potatoes or a simple garlic rice will soak up the juices and round out the meal.

FAQ

Can I use this method for chicken or pork?

Absolutely. The Vertical Gyro Pan with Skewer for Oven is versatile. For chicken, use boneless thighs stacked tightly. For pork, a marinated pork shoulder (similar to Al Pastor) works beautifully using the same vertical roasting technique.

How do I prevent the meat from spinning on the skewer?

Ensure your meat cuts are uniform and pressed firmly against one another. If you find a piece is loose, you can use a small piece of kitchen twine to tie the stack together before sliding it onto the stainless steel skewer.

Do I need to baste the meat while it cooks?

One of the best parts of using a vertical pan is the self-basting effect. The fat from the top layers drips down and coats the meat below. However, if you want an extra-dark crust, you can brush a bit of the pan drippings back onto the top layer halfway through cooking.
