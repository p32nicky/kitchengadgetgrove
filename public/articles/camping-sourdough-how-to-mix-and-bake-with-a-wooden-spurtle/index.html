Preparing Your Starter While Camping

Start your sourdough a day or two before you leave, or bring a mature starter in a sealed jar. When you’re ready to feed it:

  1. Measure equal parts flour and water (by weight) into a clean jar.
  2. Use one of the wooden spurtles to stir the mixture until it’s a smooth, homogenous batter.
  3. Scrape the sides and bottom of the jar with the spurtle’s flat edge to incorporate any dry flour patches.
  4. Cover loosely and let it sit at ambient temperature (ideally 68‑75°F) for 6‑12 hours.

The narrow profile of the spurtle makes it easy to stir in a small camping pot or even directly in the starter jar, reducing the number of dishes you need to clean.

Mixing the Dough on a Camp Stove

Once your starter is active, you can mix a simple camp‑ready sourdough:

  • Ingredients: 250 g bread flour, 150 g water, 75 g active starter, 6 g salt.
  • Step 1 – Autolyse: Combine flour and water in a lightweight pot. Stir with the wooden spurtle until no dry spots remain. Let rest 30 minutes.
  • Step 2 – Add Starter & Salt: Drop the starter in, sprinkle salt, and use the spurtle to fold the dough until it becomes cohesive.
  • Step 3 – Bulk Fermentation: Let the dough rise for 2‑3 hours, performing a series of stretch‑and‑folds every 30 minutes. The spurtle’s thin tip is perfect for lifting and folding without tearing the dough.

Because the spurtle is slim, you can perform these folds in the same pot, keeping the process tidy and portable.

Baking the Loaf Over a Campfire

When the dough has roughly doubled, shape it into a round and place it in a pre‑heated Dutch oven or cast‑iron skillet. Cover and bake over a low, steady fire for about 30 minutes, then remove the lid for another 10‑15 minutes to develop a crisp crust. Throughout the bake, the wooden spurtle can be used to:

  • Scrape any flour that sticks to the pot’s sides.
  • Gently lift the loaf for a quick check without breaking the crust.
  • Serve the finished bread by sliding the spurtle under the loaf for a clean lift.

Its non‑scratch surface ensures your cookware stays pristine for the next adventure.

Caring for Your Spurtle in the Wild

Wood needs a little TLC, even on the trail. After each use:

  1. Rinse the spurtle with warm water (no soap needed if you’re outdoors).
  2. Dry it immediately with a clean cloth to prevent warping.
  3. If you’re at a campsite with a fire pit, you can briefly toast the spurtle’s handle over low heat to kill any lingering microbes.

FAQ

Can I use the wooden spurtle with gluten‑free sourdough?

Yes. The spurtle’s smooth wood works equally well with rice, buckwheat, or sorghum flours. Just give it a quick rinse between batches.

Is the spurtle safe for use with metal cookware?

Absolutely. Its non‑metallic nature means it won’t spark or scratch stainless steel, cast iron, or aluminum pans.

How long will the wooden spurtle last?

With proper drying and occasional oiling (food‑grade mineral oil), a well‑maintained spurtle can last for years, even with frequent camping use.
