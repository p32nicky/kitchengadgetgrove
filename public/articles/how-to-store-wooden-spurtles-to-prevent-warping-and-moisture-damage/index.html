Dry Thoroughly Before You Store Wooden Spurtles to Prevent Warping

The biggest mistake most home bakers make is tossing their tools into a drawer while they are still damp. When wood stays wet, it softens, making it much more likely to bend under the weight of other utensils. Always hand-wash your spurtles—never put them in the dishwasher, as the high heat and prolonged water exposure are a recipe for disaster.

After washing, don't just lay them flat on a towel. Instead, prop them up or lean them against a backsplash to allow air to circulate around the entire surface of the wood. For a set like the Wooden Spurtle & Sourdough Starter Stirrer Set (2 PCS), which includes both a skinny spurtle and a Danish dough whisk, the whisk requires a bit more attention. Ensure water isn't pooling in the curves of the whisk, as this is where mold or warping typically begins.

Choose the Right Storage Location

Where you put your tools is just as important as how you clean them. Avoid storing your wooden utensils directly next to the stove or dishwasher. These areas are hotspots for steam and rapid temperature changes, which cause the wood fibers to expand and contract quickly, leading to splits.

If you use a utensil crock, make sure it isn't packed too tightly. When tools are jammed together, they can't breathe, and the pressure of other heavy spoons can actually push a skinny spurtle into a curved shape over time. Give your tools some breathing room so they stay straight and dry.

The Role of Seasoning and Oil

To truly store wooden spurtle prevent warping, you need to create a barrier between the wood and the moisture. This is done through "seasoning." Just like a cast-iron skillet, wooden tools benefit from a light coating of food-grade mineral oil. This fills the pores of the wood, meaning the sourdough starter won't soak as deeply into the grain.

Every few weeks, follow this simple maintenance routine:

  • Clean and completely dry your spurtle and dough whisk.
  • Apply a small amount of food-grade mineral oil or beeswax cream to a clean cloth.
  • Rub the oil into the wood, focusing on the areas that contact the dough most.
  • Let it sit for a few hours, then wipe away any excess oil with a paper towel.

This process doesn't just prevent warping; it also prevents the wood from becoming brittle and cracking during the dry winter months when indoor heating strips moisture from the air.

Managing Moisture in Sourdough Tools

Sourdough tools face a specific challenge: the acidity and moisture of the starter. If you leave your spurtle resting inside your starter jar, the end of the stick will absorb moisture faster than the handle. This uneven hydration is a primary cause of warping.

When using the Wooden Spurtle & Sourdough Starter Stirrer Set (2 PCS), treat the skinny spurtle as a tool for active mixing and scraping, not a permanent resident of the jar. Once you've stirred your starter and scraped the sides clean, remove the tool, rinse it, and store it in a dry area. This simple habit extends the life of the wood significantly.

Signs Your Wooden Tools Need Attention

It's helpful to do a quick "health check" on your tools once a month. Look for these red flags: grain raising (where the wood feels fuzzy or rough), small hairline cracks, or a slight curve in the shaft of the spurtle. If the wood feels dry or looks dull, it's time for another round of seasoning.

If you notice a slight warp starting, you can sometimes mitigate it by storing the tool in a dry, room-temperature environment and applying a generous amount of mineral oil to the "dry" side of the curve to balance the moisture levels, though severe warping is usually permanent.

FAQ

Can I put my wooden spurtle in the dishwasher?

Absolutely not. The intense heat and harsh detergents of a dishwasher strip the natural oils from the wood and force moisture deep into the grain, which almost guarantees warping and cracking.

What is the best oil to use for seasoning?

Use food-grade mineral oil. Avoid vegetable oils, olive oil, or canola oil, as these are organic fats that can go rancid over time, leaving your baking tools smelling like old oil.

How often should I oil my sourdough stirrers?

Depending on how often you bake, every 2 to 4 weeks is usually sufficient. If you notice the wood looking pale or feeling "thirsty," it's time for a fresh coat.
