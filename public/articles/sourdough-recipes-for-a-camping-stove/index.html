Classic Cast Iron Skillet Bread

>When looking for sourdough recipes for camping stove cooking, the cast iron skillet is your best friend. It retains heat well and creates a beautiful crust. This method works best with a high-hydration dough (around 75% hydration) that you have already bulk fermented at home or allowed to rise slowly in a cooler while you hike.

>When you are ready to bake, place your seasoned cast iron skillet over the burner. Let it heat up gradually on low for about 5 minutes to warm the bottom without scorching it. Carefully transfer your dough into the skillet. If you are cooking on a camp stove with a lid, put the lid on immediately to trap the steam. This mimics a Dutch oven. If you don't have a lid, you can rig a foil dome over the top, but a tight-fitting lid is superior. Bake on low for about 15-20 minutes, then remove the lid to let the crust brown and harden for another 5 minutes.

Rustic Sourdough Flatbreads

>If you are short on time or fuel, flatbreads are the way to go. These are quick, require minimal rising time at the campsite, and are perfect for dipping in soup or slathering with jam. Because the dough is thinner, you don't have to worry about the center being raw while the outside burns.

>To make these, you will follow a simple process at the campsite:

  1. Prep the Dough: Turn your active starter out onto a floured cutting board. Using the dough scraper from your set, divide the dough into tennis-ball-sized portions.
  2. Shape: Flatten each ball with your hands. You don't need a rolling pin; the palm of your hand works perfectly to stretch them into oval discs.
  3. Cook: Heat a dry skillet over medium-high heat. Once hot, slap a flatbread onto the pan.
  4. Flip: Cook for 2-3 minutes until bubbles form and the bottom has charred spots, then flip and cook the other side.

Managing Heat and Altitude

>Baking outdoors introduces variables that your home kitchen doesn't have, primarily wind and altitude. Most camping stoves do not have the precise temperature control of a gas range, so you must rely on visual cues. If your burner only has "High" and "Low," use a windscreen to stabilize the flame and prevent the heat from gusting away.

>At higher altitudes, water boils at a lower temperature, which can actually be beneficial for sourdough. You are less likely to burn the bottom of your loaf before the inside crumb sets. However, the lower heat means you may need to extend the baking time slightly. Keep the lid on as long as possible to create a humid environment, which helps the bread cook through without drying out the crust too early.

FAQ

How do I transport sourdough starter without spilling it?

The best method is to feed your starter before you leave home and let it peak in the car, or carry it in a sturdy, wide-mouthed jar with a screw-top lid filled only halfway to allow for expansion. A cooler is ideal to keep fermentation temperature stable.

Can I bake sourdough on a pocket rocket backpacking stove?

It is possible, but difficult. Small canister stoves struggle to heat a large cast iron skillet evenly and waste fuel quickly. For backpacking, stick to the flatbread method or use a lightweight aluminum pot with a tight lid to steam the bread.

Do I need to bring flour to camp?

It depends on the recipe. For the skillet bread, you can prepare the full dough at home, let it rise in the cooler, and bake it immediately at camp. For flatbreads, bringing a small bag of flour is helpful for dusting your work surface and the dough to prevent sticking.
