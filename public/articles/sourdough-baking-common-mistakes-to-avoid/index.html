What Makes Sourdough So Challenging?

When you first start baking sourdough, the process can feel like a maze. The natural leavening, the long fermentation, and the need for precise technique mean that even seasoned bakers sometimes slip. That’s why many new bakers fall into the same common mistakes in sourdough baking. Understanding these pitfalls early on can save you hours of frustration and give you a loaf that truly rises to the occasion.

1. Skipping the Autolyse

The autolyse step—mixing flour and water and letting it rest for 20–30 minutes—might sound like a waste of time, but it’s essential. It hydrates the flour, starts gluten development, and reduces the need for intense kneading. Without it, your dough can feel tight and dry, leading to uneven bubbles and a denser crumb. Remember to let that mixture sit before adding the starter and salt.

2. Using the Wrong Starter Ratio

Many beginners use too much or too little starter. A common rule of thumb is 25–30% starter relative to the total flour weight. Too little starter means the dough won’t rise properly, while too much can make it overly sour and difficult to shape. Measure your starter by weight, not volume, to keep the ratio accurate. If you’re unsure, start with 100 grams of starter for every 500 grams of flour.

3. Neglecting Temperature Control

Sourdough loves a warm, stable environment. Fluctuating temperatures can stall fermentation or cause overproofing. Aim for 75–80°F (24–27°C) during bulk fermentation. If your kitchen is cooler, place the dough in a turned-off oven with the light on, or use a proofing box. If it’s too warm, chill the dough in the fridge for a few hours to slow the process.

4. Over‑ or Under‑Kneading

Gluten development is a delicate balance. Knead just enough to achieve a smooth, elastic dough—about 8–10 minutes by hand or 5–6 minutes with a stand mixer. Over‑kneading makes the loaf tough, while under‑kneading leaves it gummy. A quick test: press a finger into the dough; if the indentation stays, you’re ready to let it rise.

5. Poor Scoring Technique

Scoring isn’t just for show—it controls the expansion of the loaf. A shallow, too wide cut can cause the bread to split unevenly, while a deep, narrow cut may restrict airflow. Use a sharp lame or bread scoring knife and make a clean, diagonal cut that’s about 1/4 to 1/3 of the loaf’s height. If you’re new to scoring, practice on a small piece of dough before committing to the final loaf.

6. Inadequate Proofing Time

Rushing the final proof can result in a loaf that’s flat and heavy. After shaping, let the dough rest in a proofing basket for 1–2 hours at room temperature, then chill it overnight if you’re short on time. The cool environment allows flavors to develop fully without the dough overproofing.

Practical Workflow: A Step‑by‑Step Checklist

  1. Measure ingredients by weight.
  2. Mix flour and water for the autolyse; let rest 30 minutes.
  3. Add starter and salt; knead until elastic.
  4. Bulk ferment in a warm spot, folding every 30 minutes.
  5. Shape loaf and proof in a basket.
  6. Score with the lame before baking.
  7. Bake at 450°F (232°C) with steam for 20 minutes, then reduce to 400°F (204°C) for 20‑25 more minutes.
  8. Cool on a rack before slicing.

Conclusion

Mastering sourdough is a journey, not a sprint. By avoiding the most frequent mistakes—skipping the autolyse, mismanaging starter ratios, ignoring temperature, over‑kneading, poor scoring, rushing proofs, and using subpar tools—you’ll see a noticeable difference in rise, crumb, and flavor. FAQ

What is the best way to keep my starter active?

Feed it daily with equal parts flour and water by weight. Store it in a loosely capped jar at room temperature, and refrigerate if you’re not baking for a few days.

Can I bake sourdough in a toaster oven?

Yes, but you’ll need to adjust the temperature and time. Use a baking stone or Dutch oven to mimic a conventional oven’s heat distribution.

How long does a sourdough loaf stay fresh?

It stays best within 24 hours. Store it in a paper bag at room temperature or wrap loosely in foil. For longer storage, freeze the loaf in a sealed bag.
