Why a Wooden Spurtle Beats Plastic or Metal for Sourdough Batter

Plastic whiskers can nick the surface of a sourdough starter, while metal can heat up and scorch the dough. A wooden spurtle, on the other hand, is naturally non‑reactive and stays cool. It’s also lightweight, making long mixing sessions less tiring. The curved handle lets you reach into the bottom of a jar or pan without splashing, which is essential when you’re working with a sticky, airy batter.

Preparing the Sourdough Starter for Crepe‑Ready Batter

Start with a mature starter—one that’s been fed regularly and is bubbly. The spurtle’s slim design makes it easy to stir the starter into your flour mixture without breaking the gluten network. Here’s a quick routine:

  1. Measure 1 cup of all‑purpose flour and ½ cup of water into a mixing bowl.
  2. Whisk in ¼ cup of active starter using the wooden spurtle.
  3. Let the batter rest for 30 minutes; the dough will expand slightly.

During the rest, use the spurtle to gently fold in a pinch of salt and a splash of milk or buttermilk to thin the batter if needed.

Cooking Tips for Fluffy, Golden Crepes and Pancakes

1. Pre‑heat the pan to medium‑high; a drop of water should sizzle.

2. Use a thin layer of oil—just a brush or a paper towel dab—to keep the batter from drying out.

3. Spread the batter evenly by tilting the pan and swirling the dough with the spurtle, which also helps distribute heat.

4. Flip only once for crepes, or twice for pancakes, using the skinny spatula to lift without tearing.

5. Serve immediately with your favorite toppings, or keep warm in a low oven.

Cleaning and Caring for Your Wooden Spurtle

Wood is porous, so avoid soaking the spurtle in hot water. Instead, rinse with warm water, wipe dry, and apply a light coat of food‑safe mineral oil every few weeks to keep it from cracking. The non‑scratch design means it won’t damage ceramic or cast‑iron pans—an added bonus for multi‑purpose use.
