2. Mixing the Levain: Why a Danish‑Style Dough Stick Matters

When you scale up to a levain (the pre‑ferment that gives sourdough its flavor), the 2 PCS Wooden Spurtle Set shines. The Danish‑style dough whisk provides enough leverage to blend a thick, sticky levain without over‑working it. Over‑mixing can break down gluten prematurely, leading to a dense crumb later.

Tip: Combine the levain ingredients in a medium bowl, then use the wooden spurtle to stir in a figure‑eight motion. This promotes even hydration and keeps the mixture from clinging to the bowl sides.

3. Autolyse and Early Dough Development

After the levain is ready, you’ll mix the final dough. Autolyse—a rest period of 30‑45 minutes—allows flour to absorb water, developing gluten naturally. During this stage, the wooden spurtle is perfect for the initial stir because it won’t tear the developing gluten network.

Method:

  • Combine flour and water in a large container.
  • Use the skinny wooden spurtle to fold the mixture until no dry flour remains.
  • Cover and let rest.

When you add the levain and salt after the autolyse, return to the spurtle for a gentle, short knead. The result is a smooth, elastic dough ready for bulk fermentation.

4. Bulk Fermentation: Stretch‑and‑Fold with Minimal Mess

During the 3‑4 hour bulk rise, you’ll perform a series of stretch‑and‑folds every 30 minutes. The wooden spurtle helps you lift the dough without tearing it, and its tapered tip scrapes the bowl clean between folds, keeping the dough hydrated evenly.

How to stretch‑and‑fold:

  1. Wet the spurtle lightly to prevent sticking.
  2. Grab the dough from the side, lift, and fold it over the center.
  3. Rotate the bowl 90° and repeat until you’ve completed four folds.

Because the spurtle is non‑scratch, you can use it directly in glass or ceramic containers without worrying about damage.

5. Shaping and Scoring: The Final Touches

When the dough has risen to about 1.5 times its original size, it’s time to shape. A quick scrape with the wooden spurtle removes excess flour from the work surface, giving you a clean canvas for shaping. After the final proof, score the loaf with a razor blade—then bake.

While the spurtle isn’t used for scoring, its role in earlier stages ensures a smooth, well‑developed dough that will accept a clean, crisp cut and expand beautifully in the oven.

FAQ

Can I use a metal spoon instead of a wooden spurtle?

Yes, but metal can scratch glass jars and may transfer heat to the starter, potentially altering fermentation speed. A wooden spurtle is gentler and non‑reactive.

How often should I replace the wooden spurtle?

With proper care, a wooden spurtle can last years. Replace it only if you notice cracks, warping, or a lingering sour smell that won’t wash out.

Is the spurtle safe for high‑heat applications?

The spurtle is intended for mixing and scraping at room temperature. It’s not designed for direct exposure to oven heat or stovetop cooking.
