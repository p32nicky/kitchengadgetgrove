Material Matters: How Wood and Plastic Behave in a Starter

Wood is naturally porous, which means it won’t conduct heat or cold the way plastic can. This helps maintain the starter’s temperature during a quick stir, especially in a chilly kitchen. Wooden spurtles also have a gentle, non‑abrasive surface that won’t scratch glass jars or silicone lids.

Plastic spatulas, on the other hand, are typically non‑porous and can feel a bit slick when wet. While they’re easy to clean and often dishwasher‑safe, some plastics can become warped over time if exposed to hot water or high‑temperature doughs. For sourdough starter – which sits at room temperature – the heat factor isn’t a big issue, but durability still matters.

Performance in the Jar: Mixing, Scraping, and Aeration

Both tools can stir a starter, but the shape and flexibility differ. A wooden spurtle is long, slender, and slightly tapered, allowing you to reach the bottom of a 500 ml jar without splashing. Its smooth wooden surface glides through the thick, bubbly mixture, helping to incorporate trapped gases without breaking them.

Plastic spatulas are often broader and flatter. They excel at scraping the sides of a container, which is useful if you’re transferring the starter to a larger mixing bowl. However, the broader shape can create more turbulence, potentially over‑mixing the starter and causing it to lose some of its natural lift.

When the goal is a gentle blend that keeps the starter’s structure intact, the wooden spurtle’s narrow profile shines. For quick, thorough scraping after a feeding, a plastic spatula can be handy, but you’ll need to be mindful not to gouge the glass.

Cleaning and Maintenance: Longevity of Your Tools

Wooden spurtles require a bit of care. After each use, rinse with warm water, pat dry, and occasionally rub a thin layer of food‑grade mineral oil to prevent cracking. This maintenance routine actually helps the wood stay supple and odor‑free, which is ideal for the aromatic environment of sourdough.

Plastic spatulas are virtually maintenance‑free: a quick rinse or a run through the dishwasher does the trick. The trade‑off is that they can retain odors if you use them for strong‑smelling doughs, and over time the plastic may become stained.

For bakers who love a low‑maintenance routine, the plastic option is attractive. For those who appreciate a tool that gets better with age and can be refreshed with simple oiling, the wooden spurtle is a rewarding choice.

Practical Tips: Getting the Most Out of Your Spurtle or Spatula

Regardless of which tool you choose, a few technique tweaks can improve your starter care:

  1. Stir gently, not vigorously. Use a circular motion with the wooden spurtle to keep the starter’s bubbles intact.
  2. Scrape the bottom. Whether using wood or plastic, angle the tool to lift any settled flour or liquid, ensuring an even feed.
  3. Transfer with a clean tool. If you need to move the starter to a larger bowl, rinse the spurtle or spatula between batches to avoid cross‑contamination.

These habits help maintain a healthy microbial balance and reduce the chance of off‑flavors developing.

FAQ

Can I use a wooden spurtle for other doughs besides sourdough?

Absolutely. The slender design works well for rye, whole‑grain, and even wet batter mixes. Just rinse and dry after each use.

Is it safe to put a wooden spurtle in the dishwasher?

No. Prolonged exposure to high heat and water can cause wood to warp or crack. Hand‑wash, then dry and oil occasionally.

Will a plastic spatula affect the flavor of my starter?

Plastic itself doesn’t impart flavor, but if the spatula has absorbed strong odors from previous uses, it could transfer subtle hints. Keeping it clean helps avoid this.
