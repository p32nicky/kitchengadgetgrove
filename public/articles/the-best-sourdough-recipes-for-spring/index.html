3 Must-Try Sourdough Recipes for Spring

Spring’s bounty of fresh herbs, vegetables, and fruits deserves a starring role in your sourdough. Here are three recipes to try:

  • Herbed Sourdough with Fresh Chives and Dill: Mix active sourdough starter with whole wheat flour, then fold in chopped herbs for a fragrant, earthy loaf. Perfect with a light dusting of sea salt.
  • Spring Vegetable Sourdough: Fold in sautéed asparagus, fava beans, and caramelized leeks for a savory twist. Use olive oil in the dough for extra moisture.
  • Rhubarb-Lavender Sourdough: Add dried rhubarb and a pinch of culinary lavender to a high-hydration rye sourdough. Brush with honey after baking for a sweet, floral finish.

Perfecting the Crust: Why Scoring Matters

A crisp, golden crust is every sourdough baker’s goal. The bread scoring lame from the 3-in-1 Sourdough Whisk Set lets you make clean, precise cuts that control how the loaf expands in the oven. For spring loaves with delicate herbs or fruits, shallow diagonal scores prevent cracking and let the flavors shine. Pro tip: Score just before baking for maximum oven spring.

Seasonal Baking Tips for Sourdough Success

  1. Adjust hydration: Spring’s humidity affects dough consistency. Start with slightly less water than usual and adjust as you mix.
  2. Feed your starter early: Warmer weather speeds up fermentation. Feed your starter 4-6 hours before baking for peak activity.
  3. Use cold fermentation: For complex flavor, shape dough and refrigerate overnight before baking. The chill slows the rise and deepens herbal or fruit notes.

FAQ

Can I use all-purpose flour in these spring sourdough recipes?

Absolutely! For a lighter texture, blend all-purpose with whole wheat or rye flour. The 3-in-1 Sourdough Whisk Set helps incorporate air for a fluffier crumb.

How do I shape dough without overworking it?

Use the dough scraper from your whisk set to gently fold and transfer dough. Avoid excessive kneading—spring’s warmth speeds gluten development naturally.

Yes. Warmer temperatures boost activity—feed every 4-6 hours or refrigerate between feedings if you’re not baking daily.
