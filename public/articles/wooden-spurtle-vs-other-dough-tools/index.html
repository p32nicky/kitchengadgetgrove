Wooden Spurtle vs Metal Whisk

Metal whisks are great for incorporating air into batters, but they’re overkill for a dense sourdough starter. The wires can tear the gluten network, leading to a weaker loaf. A wooden spurtle, on the other hand, gently folds the starter, preserving the natural bubbles you’ve cultivated. Additionally, metal whisks can snag on the lip of a jar, making it harder to scrape the last few drops of starter. The wooden spurtle’s smooth surface slides in and out with no resistance.

Practical Tips for Using Your Wooden Spurtle

Here’s how to get the most out of the spurtle set, whether you’re feeding a starter or mixing a whole‑grain dough:

  1. Dry the wood after each use. Wipe it clean with a damp cloth, then let it air‑dry. Avoid soaking it; prolonged moisture can cause warping.
  2. Season the wood occasionally. Rub a thin layer of food‑grade mineral oil into the surface once a month. This keeps the wood from drying out and makes cleaning easier.
  3. Use the skinny stick for starter feeding. Insert it into the jar, stir in the fresh flour and water, and scrape the sides to pull down any clinging bits.
  4. Switch to the Danish‑style whisk for heavier doughs. Its slightly wider shape helps break up lumps without over‑mixing.

FAQ

Can I use a wooden spurtle for high‑heat cooking?

It’s best to keep the spurtle away from direct heat. The wood can char if left in a hot pan, which may affect flavor and durability. Use it for mixing, scraping, and stirring at room temperature or with warm (but not boiling) ingredients.

How do I prevent my wooden spurtle from cracking?

Regularly oil the surface with food‑grade mineral oil and avoid soaking it in water. Store it in a dry place and don’t leave it in a damp sink overnight.

Is the wooden spurtle dishwasher safe?

No. The high humidity and heat of a dishwasher can cause the wood to warp and split. Hand‑wash with a mild detergent, rinse, and dry promptly.
