Assessing Your Starter: Is it Actually Dead?

Before you start the revival process, check for signs of life. If you see a clear or dark liquid on top (called "hooch"), that's actually a great sign. Hooch is a byproduct of fermentation; it means your yeast is hungry, not dead. Simply pour it off or stir it back in.

The only time you should truly toss a starter is if you see fuzzy mold (white, green, or black) or streaks of bright pink or orange. If it just looks dormant and smells like strong vinegar or nail polish remover, it is perfectly salvageable with a few disciplined feedings.

The Step-by-Step Revival Process

The goal is to lower the acidity and provide fresh nutrients to wake up the yeast. Instead of just adding flour on top, you need to thoroughly integrate the new feed into the old culture.

  1. Discard: Remove all but about 25-50 grams of your starter. This prevents the volume from becoming unmanageable and reduces the acidity that can inhibit yeast growth.
  2. Feed: Add an equal weight of room temperature filtered water and unbleached all-purpose or bread flour.
  3. Mix: Use the skinny wooden spurtle spatula from the Wooden Spurtle & Sourdough Starter Stirrer Set. Its slim profile allows you to reach the very bottom and sides of the jar, ensuring no dry flour pockets remain.
  4. Wait: Leave the jar in a warm spot (70-75°F) for 12 to 24 hours.
  5. Repeat: Repeat this process every 24 hours until the starter consistently doubles in size within 4-8 hours of feeding.

Pro Tips for Maintaining a Healthy Culture

To avoid having to revive your starter in the future, consistency is key. If you aren't baking daily, keep your starter in the fridge and feed it once a week. When you're ready to bake, take it out two days early and give it two room-temperature feedings to peak its activity.

Always use filtered water. Chlorine in tap water can sometimes stunt the growth of wild yeast, making the revival process take longer than necessary. If you notice your starter is sluggish, try a "booster feed" using a small amount of whole rye flour; the extra nutrients often act like a shot of espresso for dormant yeast.

FAQ

How long does it take to revive a dormant starter?

Depending on how long it was neglected, it typically takes 3 to 7 days of consistent feedings to see the starter doubling reliably again. Be patient and keep the environment warm.

Can I use a metal spoon instead of a wooden spurtle?

Yes, you can, but wooden tools are generally preferred in sourdough baking because they don't conduct heat as quickly and won't scratch your jars. A spurtle specifically is better for narrow jars than a standard spoon.

What if my starter isn't bubbling after three days?

Check your water temperature and the warmth of your kitchen. If it's too cold, the yeast won't activate. Try placing your jar inside an oven with the light turned on (but the oven off) to create a cozy, warm environment.
