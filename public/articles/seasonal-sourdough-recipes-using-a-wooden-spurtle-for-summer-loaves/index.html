Preparing the Starter: The Spurtle’s First Job

Before you even think about shaping loaves, you need a healthy starter. Here’s a quick routine that the wooden spurtle excels at:

  1. Combine 60 g whole‑wheat flour and 60 g water in a glass jar.
  2. Use the skinny spurtle to stir until the mixture is smooth—no lumps.
  3. Cover loosely and let sit 12‑24 hours at room temperature.
  4. Feed with equal parts flour and water every 12 hours, stirring each time with the spurtle to incorporate oxygen.

When the starter is bubbly and has doubled in size, it’s ready. The wooden spurtle’s non‑scratch surface ensures you can scrape the sides clean, capturing every active cell of yeast for the best rise.

Herb‑Infused Summer Loaf

This loaf is bright, aromatic, and perfect for a backyard picnic.

  • 200 g active starter
  • 350 g bread flour
  • 150 g water (adjust for humidity)
  • 10 g salt
  • 2 tbsp chopped fresh rosemary
  • 1 tbsp chopped fresh thyme

Mix the flour, water, and starter with the wooden spurtle until a shaggy dough forms. Let it rest 30 minutes (autolyse), then add salt and herbs, folding them in with the spurtle. After a series of stretch‑and‑folds over 2 hours, shape and proof. Bake at 475°F on a preheated stone for 25 minutes. The wooden spurtle’s slender profile lets you fold herbs evenly without tearing the dough.

Citrus‑Zest Sourdough

Bright lemon or orange zest adds a summer sparkle to the classic sourdough tang.

Ingredients:

  • 200 g starter
  • 340 g all‑purpose flour
  • 160 g water
  • 10 g salt
  • 2 tbsp finely grated lemon zest

Combine flour, water, and starter using the spurtle, then let rest 20 minutes. Sprinkle salt and zest, and incorporate with gentle turns of the spurtle. The wooden surface prevents the zest oils from sticking to the tool, keeping the dough smooth. After bulk fermentation, shape into a round, let it proof, and bake at 450°F for 30 minutes. The result is a fragrant loaf with a subtle citrus bite—ideal for summer brunch.

Tomato‑Basil Sourdough for Picnic Sandwiches

Sun‑dried tomatoes and fresh basil give this loaf a Mediterranean vibe.

Ingredients:

  • 200 g starter
  • 330 g bread flour
  • 170 g water
  • 10 g salt
  • 1 /4 cup chopped sun‑dried tomatoes (packed in oil, drained)
  • 2 tbsp chopped basil

Mix flour, water, and starter with the wooden spurtle. After the autolyse, fold in salt, tomatoes, and basil using the spurtle’s narrow tip to distribute evenly without crushing the delicate basil leaves. Perform four stretch‑and‑folds over 90 minutes, then shape into an oblong loaf. Bake at 460°F for 28 minutes. This loaf holds up well to hearty fillings and stays fresh through a sunny day.

Tips for Using Your Wooden Spurtle All Summer Long

1. Keep it dry. After each use, wipe the spurtle clean with a damp cloth and let it air‑dry. Wood absorbs moisture, which can lead to warping.

2. Season occasionally. Lightly rub a food‑safe oil (such as walnut or almond) into the wood every few weeks to maintain its smoothness and prevent cracking.

3. Use it for jar scraping. The spurtle’s thin edge excels at retrieving every last drop of starter from glass containers—no waste, no mess.

4. Don’t over‑heat. While the spurtle is safe for dough mixing, avoid exposing it to direct oven heat for extended periods; it’s designed for prep, not baking.

FAQ

Can I use the wooden spurtle with gluten‑free sourdough?

Yes. The spurtle’s non‑reactive surface works with any flour type. Just be aware that gluten‑free doughs can be stickier; a quick dip in water helps the spurtle glide smoothly.

How often should I replace my wooden spurtle?

With proper care—drying after each use and occasional oiling—a high‑quality wooden spurtle can last years. Replace it only if it cracks or develops a strong odor.
