Recipe 1: Classic Sourdough Toast with Avocado & Poached Egg

Ingredients

  • 1 cup active sourdough starter (fed 4‑6 hours ago)
  • 300 g bread flour
  • 180 ml water
  • 5 g salt
  • 1 ripe avocado
  • 2 large eggs
  • Crushed red pepper flakes, optional

Method

  1. Mix starter, flour, and water in a large bowl. Use the Wooden Spurtle to blend until no dry bits remain. Let sit 30 minutes (autolyse).
  2. Add salt and fold the dough a few times with a wet hand. Let rise until doubled, about 3‑4 hours.
  3. Shape a round loaf, place in a proofing basket, and refrigerate overnight.
  4. Next morning, preheat a Dutch oven to 450°F, bake 20 minutes with lid on, then 15 minutes uncovered.
  5. Slice, toast the slices, mash avocado on top, and place a poached egg on each. Sprinkle pepper flakes if desired.

The spurtle’s slender shape is perfect for folding the dough during the short bulk fermentation, ensuring a uniform crumb that toasts evenly.

Recipe 2: Sourdough Breakfast Muffins with Cheddar & Chives

These muffins are a handheld version of sourdough goodness, great for on‑the‑go mornings.

Ingredients

  • 1 cup sourdough starter
  • 250 g all‑purpose flour
  • 120 ml milk
  • 1 egg
  • 1 tsp baking powder
  • 1 cup shredded cheddar
  • 2 tbsp chopped chives
  • Pinch of salt

Method

  1. Whisk starter, milk, and egg together with the Wooden Spurtle. The thin tip helps break up any clumps.
  2. Stir in flour, baking powder, and salt until just combined. Fold in cheese and chives.
  3. Divide batter into a greased muffin tin (12 cups) and bake at 375°F for 18‑20 minutes.

The spurtle’s non‑metal surface prevents the batter from developing a metallic taste, which can happen with stainless steel when dairy is involved.

Recipe 3: Sweet Sourdough Breakfast Bruschetta

Combine the tang of sourdough with honey‑drizzled fruit for a quick, elegant treat.

Ingredients

  • 1 loaf sourdough bread (from a starter you’ve tended with a spurtle)
  • 2 tbsp butter, softened
  • 1 cup mixed berries
  • 2 tbsp honey
  • Fresh mint leaves for garnish

Method

  1. Slice the loaf into ½‑inch thick pieces and toast until golden.
  2. Spread each slice with butter while still warm.
  3. Top with berries tossed in honey, then garnish with mint.

The wooden spurtle comes in handy when you’re mixing the berries and honey in a small bowl—its smooth surface won’t crush the fruit, preserving texture.

Practical Tips for Using a Wooden Spurtle with Your Starter

1. Keep it dry between uses. After stirring, wipe the spurtle with a clean cloth and let it air‑dry. This prevents wood rot and keeps flavors pure.

2. Don’t soak it. Unlike silicone, wood doesn’t like prolonged water exposure. If it gets too wet, rinse quickly and dry immediately.

3. Season occasionally. Lightly rub the spurtle with a food‑safe mineral oil once a month. This restores sheen and reduces absorption of sourdough odors.

4. Use the tapered tip for jar scraping. When feeding your starter, the spurtle’s tip slides into the narrow mouth of a mason jar, ensuring you retrieve every last drop of starter—essential for maintaining a consistent feeding schedule.

5. Reserve the spurtle for sourdough only. Using it for other sticky batters can transfer flavors. Keep a dedicated set for starter work and you’ll notice a cleaner, more predictable rise.

FAQ

Can I use a wooden spurtle for high‑hydration dough?

Yes. Its sturdy, narrow shape lets you fold and turn a 80‑90% hydration dough without tearing the gluten network. Just work quickly to keep the dough from sticking.

Is the spurtle safe for dishwasher cleaning?

It’s best to avoid the dishwasher. High heat and harsh detergents can warp the wood. Hand‑wash with mild soap, rinse, and dry.

How often should I feed my sourdough starter when using a spurtle?

Feed every 12‑24 hours depending on temperature. The spurtle helps you scrape the jar clean, ensuring each feed gets the full amount of starter and no dry crust forms on the sides.
