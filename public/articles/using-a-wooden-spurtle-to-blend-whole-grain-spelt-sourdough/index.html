Why a Wooden Spurtle is a Game‑Changer for Whole Grain Spelt Sourdough

Whole grain spelt sourdough is a labor of love. The dense, nutty flour clumps and the starter’s natural activity demand a gentle touch. A wooden spurtle—long, skinny, and slightly curved—offers just that. Unlike metal whisks, it won’t scorch the dough, and its flexible blade keeps the gluten from over‑working. When you pair it with a sturdy wooden spatula, you get a two‑in‑one set that handles mixing, scraping, and shaping with ease.

Getting Started: Prepare Your Starter and Dough

Before you bring out the spurtle, make sure your starter is active—bubbling and smelling pleasantly yeasty. Mix the starter with the whole grain spelt flour, water, and a pinch of salt in a large bowl. The dough should be sticky but hold together when pressed.

Gently fold the dough from the edges toward the center, turning it 180 degrees after each fold. This technique distributes the starter evenly without tearing the gluten network.

Scraping the Jar: Keep Every Grain

One of the biggest frustrations with sourdough is losing dough in the jar. The wooden spurtle’s narrow tip is perfect for scraping every last crumb. Slide it around the inside walls, then tilt the jar to let gravity do the rest. This ensures you’re not wasting the precious whole‑grain flour that gives spelt its signature flavor.

Mixing Tips: How Much Force is Needed?

Whole grain spelt flour can be heavy. The wooden spurtle’s light, flexible shaft allows you to apply just enough pressure to incorporate air without over‑kneading. Here’s a quick checklist:

  • Use a light, circular motion.
  • Stop once the dough becomes cohesive.
  • Let the dough rest for 20–30 minutes before the next fold.
  • Repeat the fold‑and‑rest cycle 3–4 times.

When the dough feels elastic and slightly tacky, it’s ready for the final rise. The wooden spatula from the set can be used to transfer the dough into a banneton or a proofing basket without damaging the structure.

From Mixer to Oven: The Final Touches

After the bulk fermentation, shape the dough using the wooden spatula. The flat edge helps create a smooth surface and a neat seam. Place the loaf on a parchment‑lined baking sheet or in a Dutch oven. A quick brush of water around the edges locks in moisture, producing a crisp crust.

When the loaf is baked, the wooden tools remain useful. The spurtle can be used to gently stir the baking dough if you’re experimenting with adding seeds or nuts. The spatula’s non‑scratch surface is safe for scraping the pan, preserving your cookware.

Cleaning and Care: Keep Your Spurtle in Top Shape

Wood is porous, so proper maintenance is key. Rinse the spurtle immediately after use, then pat dry with a clean towel. Avoid soaking in hot water or using soap—this can dry out the wood. Store the set in a dry place, and occasionally rub a little food‑safe mineral oil to keep the surface supple. With proper care, your wooden spurtle will last for years of sourdough adventures.

FAQ

Can I use a wooden spurtle for other doughs?

Absolutely. Its flexibility makes it ideal for pizza dough, bread, and even cake batter. Just remember to clean it thoroughly between uses.

Will the wooden spurtle scratch my baking pans?

No. The natural wood is gentle on non‑stick and stainless steel surfaces, so you can scrape away leftover dough without worry.

Is the wooden spurtle safe for use with hot dough?

Yes, but it can warp if exposed to extreme heat for prolonged periods. Use it for mixing and scraping, and switch to a heat‑resistant silicone whisk for any high‑temperature tasks.
