Day 1: The First Feed

Combine 60 g of whole‑grain flour with 60 g of water in your jar. Stir vigorously with the wooden spurtle until you have a thick, pancake‑like batter. The spurtle’s narrow profile helps you lift the flour from the bottom, ensuring an even mixture. Cover the jar loosely and place it in a spot that stays around 70°F (21°C) for 24 hours.

Day 2–4: Feeding and Observing

Each day, discard half of the starter (about 60 g) and feed it with another 60 g flour and 60 g water. Use the wooden spurtle to scrape the jar clean before adding the fresh feed; this removes any dried crust that could hinder the yeast and bacteria. You’ll notice bubbles forming and a pleasant, tangy aroma emerging. If the mixture looks dry, add a splash more water; if it’s too runny, a bit more flour will bring it back to a thick, stir‑able consistency.

Why a Wooden Spurtle Beats Plastic

Plastic spoons can retain odors and develop micro‑scratches that harbor unwanted microbes. Plus, the wood warms slightly in your hand, giving you tactile feedback that helps you gauge the starter’s texture.

Day 5–7: Ready for Baking

By the end of the first week, the starter should double in size within 4‑6 hours after a feed, have a frothy surface, and emit a pleasant sour smell. Perform the “float test”: drop a teaspoon of starter into a bowl of water—if it floats, it’s aerated enough for baking. When the starter is ready, you can keep feeding it daily or store it in the refrigerator and feed it once a week. Use the wooden spurtle to stir the starter before each feed; its non‑scratch surface makes jar cleaning a breeze, and you’ll never miss the last pockets of active culture.

Tips for Long‑Term Success

• Temperature control: Keep the starter at a consistent room temperature; large fluctuations can delay activity.

• Flour variety: Switching between whole‑grain and white flour can diversify the microbial community, but always use the spurtle to mix thoroughly.

• Jar hygiene: After each feeding, rinse the jar with warm water and give it a quick scrub with the wooden spurtle. The wood’s natural oils help lift residue without scratching the glass.

• Stirring rhythm: A steady, clockwise motion for about 30 seconds each feed ensures oxygen is evenly distributed, encouraging healthy yeast growth.

FAQ

Can I use a metal spoon instead of the wooden spurtle?

You can, but metal can scratch glass and may retain heat, which can stress the starter. The wooden spurtle offers a gentle, non‑scratch alternative that also helps you scrape the bottom of the jar clean.

How often should I feed my starter if I keep it in the fridge?

When refrigerated, feed the starter once a week. Take it out, discard half, feed with equal parts flour and water, stir with the wooden spurtle, let it sit at room temperature for a few hours, then return it to the fridge.

What if my starter develops a pink or orange hue?

A pink or orange tint indicates contamination. Discard the starter and begin again with fresh flour, water, and a clean jar. Using a dedicated wooden spurtle for each starter batch helps prevent cross‑contamination.
