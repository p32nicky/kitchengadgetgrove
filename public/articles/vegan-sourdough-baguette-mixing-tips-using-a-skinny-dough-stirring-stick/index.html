Why the Right Stirring Tool Matters for Vegan Sourdough

Vegan sourdough baguettes typically require a higher hydration level to achieve an open crumb. This means the dough is often tacky and clings to everything. If you use a standard spoon, you end up with a massive glob of starter stuck to the tool, wasting precious ingredients.

Mixing Your Starter for Maximum Lift

Before you even think about shaping a baguette, your starter needs to be peaked and bubbly. Mixing your feedings can be a chore if you're fighting with a tool that's too wide for your jar. Using a skinny dough stirring stick allows you to whip air into the starter efficiently.

I recommend a "stir and scrape" technique: use the spurtle to stir in a circular motion, then use the edge of the stick to scrape the interior walls of the jar. This prevents the buildup of dried starter crusts, which can harbor unwanted mold or bacteria. A clean jar means a healthy starter, and a healthy starter means a baguette that actually grows in the oven.

Once your starter is active, it's time to mix the final dough. For baguettes, you want to avoid aggressive kneading early on to preserve the gases produced by the wild yeast. Instead, use a series of folds and gentle stirs.

Pro Tips for Shaping and Baking

After your bulk fermentation, the shaping process is where the baguette gets its identity. To ensure your vegan sourdough baguettes don't flatten into pancakes, follow these steps:

  • Pre-shape gently: Gently roll the dough into a cylinder and let it rest for 20 minutes. This relaxes the gluten.
  • Use a Couche: Place your shaped loaves in a heavily floured linen cloth (couche) to support the sides during the final proof.
  • Create Steam: Place a cast iron pan with boiling water at the bottom of your oven. Steam is the secret to that glossy, thin, professional crust.
  • Score Deep: Use a sharp lame or razor blade to make overlapping diagonal cuts. This directs the "oven spring" upward.

Maintaining Your Wooden Baking Tools

Wooden tools are superior for sourdough because they don't react with the acidity of the starter and they don't scratch your glassware. However, they do require a bit of love. Never put your wooden spurtle or dough whisk in the dishwasher, as the high heat can warp the wood and cause it to crack.

Simply rinse them with warm, soapy water immediately after use. If you notice the wood starting to look dry or feeling rough, rub a small amount of food-grade mineral oil into the grain. This creates a moisture barrier that prevents dough from sticking and extends the life of your tools for years of baking.

FAQ

Can I use a metal spoon instead of a wooden spurtle?

While you can, metal can sometimes react with the lactic acid in a long-fermenting sourdough starter. Additionally, metal spoons often scratch the bottom of glass jars. A wooden spurtle is non-scratch and chemically inert, making it safer for long-term fermentation.

What is the difference between a spurtle and a dough whisk?

A spurtle is a skinny, versatile stirring stick ideal for jars and smaller mixes. A dough whisk (like the Danish-style one) has a looped wire or wood design meant to cut through thick batters and heavy doughs more efficiently than a solid stick.

Why is my vegan sourdough baguette not rising?

This is usually due to an underactive starter or insufficient steam in the oven. Ensure your starter is doubling in size within 4-6 hours before mixing. Also, make sure you are scoring the dough deeply enough to allow it to expand rapidly during the first few minutes of baking.
