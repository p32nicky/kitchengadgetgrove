Why a Gluten‑Free Bagel Deserves a Specialized Tool

Gluten‑free bagels are notorious for being dense, dry, or crumbly. The trick is in how you handle the dough—mixing it gently yet thoroughly, scraping every corner of the bowl, and keeping the flour from clumping. That’s where a gluten free bagel wooden spurtle steps in. Its narrow, tapered shape fits into the tight spaces of a mixing bowl, allowing you to stir, fold, and scrape without overworking the gluten‑free mix. The result? A chewy, flavorful bagel that rivals the store‑bought variety.

  • 2 cups gluten‑free all‑purpose flour blend (with xanthan gum)
  • 1 tsp instant yeast
  • 1 tsp sugar (or honey)
  • 1 ½ cups warm water (110°F)
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 1 tbsp olive oil or melted butter
  • Optional: sesame seeds, poppy seeds, or dried herbs for topping
  • 2 PCS Wooden Spurtle Spatula for Sourdough Starter, Wood Danish Dough Whisk (the skinny dough stirring stick)

Step‑by‑Step: Mixing with the Wooden Spurtle

1. Activate the yeast. In a small bowl, dissolve sugar in warm water. Sprinkle yeast on top, let sit 5 minutes until frothy. 2. Combine dry ingredients. Whisk flour, xanthan gum (if not pre‑mixed), and salt in a large bowl. 3. Introduce the wet mix. Pour the yeast mixture and olive oil into the dry blend. Use the wooden spurtle to stir gently—its thin blade slides under the surface, pulling flour in without tearing the dough. 4. Fold in the dough. Once the mixture starts to come together, flip the bowl over and fold the dough over itself with the spurtle. Repeat 3–4 times. The spurtle’s shape makes it easy to lift the dough from the sides and integrate it fully. 5. Scrape the bowl. When the dough is soft, use the spurtle to scrape down the sides, ensuring every grain of flour is incorporated. This step eliminates dry pockets that could lead to uneven texture.

Proofing and Shaping Your Bagels

1. First rise. Cover the bowl with a damp cloth and let the dough rise for 1 hour at room temperature. The gluten‑free dough will puff but not rise dramatically. 2. Divide and shape. Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Using the spurtle, gently press it between the dough and your palm to form a smooth ball. Divide into 6 equal pieces. 3. Create the classic ring. Pinch the center of each ball to form a hole. The spurtle’s slender tip helps you adjust the diameter without tearing the dough. 4. Second rise. Place shaped bagels on a parchment‑lined tray, cover loosely, and let rest 30 minutes. The dough will expand slightly, developing that iconic bagel texture.

Baking Tips for Optimal Texture

1. Preheat the oven. 425°F (220°C) is ideal. A baking stone or steel will give the bagels a crisp crust. 2. Boil for authenticity. Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. Add a tablespoon of sugar or a splash of malt syrup. Drop bagels 2–3 at a time, boiling 30–45 seconds per side. The wooden spurtle can double as a gentle stirrer in the boiling water, keeping the bagels from sticking together. 3. Bake. Transfer boiled bagels to the oven, sprinkle toppings, and bake 12–15 minutes until golden brown. 4. Cool properly. Let the bagels cool on a rack for at least 20 minutes before slicing. This step lets the interior set and the crust firm up.

FAQ

Can I use the spurtle for other gluten‑free doughs?

Absolutely. The narrow blade works well for pizza dough, biscuits, and even chocolate chip cookie dough. It’s especially handy for doughs that need gentle folding.

How do I clean and store the wooden spurtle?

Wipe it with a damp cloth after use, then dry immediately. Store in a dry place; avoid prolonged exposure to moisture to prevent warping.

Does the wooden spurtle affect the flavor of the bagels?

No. Wood is inert, so it won’t impart any taste. It simply provides a smooth surface for mixing and scraping.
