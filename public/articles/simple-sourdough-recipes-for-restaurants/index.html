1. Building a Reliable Starter

A strong starter is the foundation of every great sourdough loaf. Keep it active by feeding it once a day (or twice if you’re in a hot climate). Use a simple 1:1:1 ratio: equal parts starter, flour, and water by weight. A 100‑g starter will grow to 200 g after a feed—enough to leaven a 1‑kg loaf.

2. Bulk Fermentation: Timing & Temperature

Most restaurants run on a 12‑hour bulk fermentation. Place the dough in a lightly oiled bowl, cover, and let it sit at 75–78°F (24–26°C). If you’re working in a cooler kitchen, add a heat lamp or a small oven set to 200°F (93°C) with the door ajar. Keep an eye on the dough; it should roughly double in volume.

  • First 3 hours: perform 4 sets of stretch‑and‑folds, 30 minutes apart.
  • Next 3 hours: let the dough rest; no folds.
  • Final 6 hours: shape and proof.

3. Quick & Easy Menu‑Ready Recipes

These recipes are designed for high‑volume kitchens. They use the same starter, same bulk process, but finish in under an hour after proofing.

  1. Classic San Diego Sourdough
    • Flour: 500 g bread flour
    • Water: 350 g (70% hydration)
    • Starter: 100 g (20% of flour weight)
    • Salt: 10 g

    Shape into a round, proof for 2 h, then bake at 475°F (245°C) on a preheated stone for 25 min. Finish with a drizzle of olive oil.

  2. Whole‑Grain Sourdough Flatbread
    • Flour: 250 g whole wheat, 250 g rye
    • Water: 400 g (80% hydration)
    • Starter: 100 g
    • Salt: 8 g

    Roll into 1‑inch thick sheets, bake at 425°F (220°C) for 12 min. Serve with a tangy yogurt dip.

  3. Cheese‑Crusted Sourdough Baguette
    • Flour: 600 g bread flour
    • Water: 360 g (60% hydration)
    • Starter: 120 g
    • Salt: 12 g
    • Cheese: shredded Gruyère, 50 g

    Shape into baguettes, brush with a beaten egg, sprinkle cheese, and bake at 450°F (232°C) for 30 min.

FAQ

What’s the difference between a spurtle and a regular wooden spoon?

A spurtle has a narrower, longer shaft, which makes it easier to reach the back of bowls and jars without scratching surfaces. It’s ideal for sourdough because the dough is sticky and needs a smooth tool.

Can I use the wooden whisk for other doughs?

Absolutely. The Danish dough whisk works well for pizza dough, cookie dough, and even for whipping egg whites if you need a gentle stir.

How long does a sourdough starter last in the refrigerator?

When stored at 40°F (4°C), a starter can go 2–4 weeks without feeding. Feed it once a week if you plan to keep it long‑term. Use the wooden spurtle to mix and scrape the starter before feeding.
