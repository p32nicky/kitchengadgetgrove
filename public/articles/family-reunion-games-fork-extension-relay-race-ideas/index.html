Setting Up the Course: Simple, Safe, and Scalable

Choose a location that’s open and flat—your backyard lawn, a patio, or even the living room if you’re indoors. Mark the start and finish lines with cones, ribbons, or a line of napkins. Keep the distance short enough for quick turns but long enough for the fork to feel impressive—about 20–30 feet works well for most families. If you have kids, lower the stakes by reducing the distance or adding a “no‑fork‑touch” zone.

Safety first: Make sure the path is clear of obstacles and that participants know they can’t run too fast. The fork’s tip is blunt, but it can still pinch fingers if mishandled, so a quick safety briefing is essential.

How the Relay Works: Rules That Keep It Fun

  1. Each team picks one fork. The fork must be fully extended—no half‑way stretching.
  2. Carry the fork from start to finish. The fork must remain in hand the entire time.
  3. Switch forks at the next station. At the finish line, the runner hands the fork to the next teammate, who must immediately extend it again.
  4. First team to finish wins. Tie breakers can be decided by a quick “food‑stealing” contest.

Optional twist: Each team must bring a “prank” dish—think giant sandwich, oversized cookie, or a plate of spaghetti—so that the fork’s reach is put to real use.

Practical Tips for a Smooth Race

  • Test the forks. Before the event, extend each fork to its maximum to ensure no jams.
  • Use a lightweight but sturdy base. A wooden crate or a stack of sturdy crates keeps the fork from slipping.
  • Have a backup fork. In case one breaks, a spare keeps the competition alive.
  • Encourage teamwork. The relay format naturally builds camaraderie—pair a grandparent with a grandkid for intergenerational fun.
  • Celebrate all participants. After the race, hand out “prank champion” certificates to everyone.
