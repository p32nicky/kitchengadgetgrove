The Golden Rule: Never Use the Dishwasher

It is tempting to toss everything into the dishwasher after a messy baking session, but the dishwasher is the enemy of wooden kitchenware. The combination of high-heat drying cycles and harsh detergents strips the natural oils from the wood and forces moisture deep into the grain.

When wood expands and contracts rapidly in a dishwasher, it develops micro-cracks. In a sourdough environment, these cracks can trap flour and starter remnants, creating a breeding ground for bacteria. Always hand-wash your spurtle and dough whisk immediately after use with warm, soapy water.

Daily Cleaning and Drying Techniques

For daily wooden spurtle care maintenance, keep it simple. Use a mild dish soap and a soft sponge. If you've been mixing a particularly sticky sourdough starter with your skinny wooden spurtle spatula, let it soak in warm water for just a few minutes—don't leave it submerged for hours, as this can cause the wood to warp.

The drying process is just as important as the washing. Instead of leaving your tools to dry flat on a towel where moisture can pool, prop them up or hang them. This allows air to circulate around the entire surface, ensuring the wood dries evenly and quickly.

Seasoning Your Tools with Food-Grade Oil

Over time, wood loses its natural oils, leaving it looking dull and feeling "fuzzy" to the touch. This is when you need to season your tools. Seasoning creates a hydrophobic barrier that prevents food particles and moisture from penetrating the wood.

To season your Wooden Spurtle & Sourdough Starter Stirrer Set (2 PCS), follow these steps:

  • Ensure the tools are completely clean and bone-dry.
  • Apply a generous amount of food-grade mineral oil or a beeswax-based butcher block conditioner. Avoid vegetable or olive oils, as these can go rancid over time.
  • Rub the oil into the grain using a clean cloth, covering every inch of the handle and the mixing head.
  • Let the oil soak in for several hours or overnight.
  • Wipe away any excess oil with a paper towel before your next use.

Managing Odors and Stains

Wood is porous, meaning it can occasionally pick up the scent of strong ingredients. While sourdough starter has a pleasant tang, other uses for your spurtle—like stirring garlic-heavy sauces—might leave a lingering aroma. To neutralize this, rub the wood with a halved lemon and a sprinkle of coarse salt.

The acidity of the lemon helps break down odors, while the salt acts as a gentle abrasive to lift surface stains. Rinse thoroughly and follow up with a light coating of mineral oil to restore the moisture lost during the citrus treatment.

Storage Tips for Long-Term Durability

Where you store your tools affects their lifespan. Avoid storing your spurtle directly next to the stove or oven, as extreme temperature fluctuations can cause the wood to crack. A dedicated utensil crock or a hanging rack is ideal.

If you have a set that includes both a skinny spurtle and a Danish-style dough stick, ensure they aren't crammed too tightly together. Giving them a bit of breathing room prevents moisture from being trapped between the tools, which keeps your baking kit hygienic and ready for your next levain.

FAQ

How often should I oil my wooden spurtle?

As a general rule, season your tools every 1 to 3 months, depending on how often you use them. If the wood starts to look pale or feels rough to the touch, it's time for another coat of food-grade mineral oil.

Can I use a wooden spurtle for hot soups?

Yes, wooden spurtles are excellent for hot liquids because they don't conduct heat like metal. Just be sure to avoid leaving them resting against the side of a scorching hot pot for extended periods, as this can scorch the wood.

What do I do if my spurtle develops a small crack?

If the crack is superficial, frequent oiling can prevent it from worsening. However, if the crack is deep enough to trap food particles, it's safer to replace the tool to avoid bacterial growth.
