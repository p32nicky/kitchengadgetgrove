Feeding a large group is one of the most rewarding aspects of home cooking, but it presents unique challenges, especially when working with wild yeast. If you are looking to impress your guests with deep, complex flavors, finding the right sourdough recipes for a crowd is the first step toward a successful event. Unlike quick yeasted breads, sourdough requires timing and strategy, but the payoff in texture and taste is unmatched. Whether you are hosting a casual brunch or a formal dinner, planning your bake around bulk preparation and efficient tools will save your sanity.

Strategy for Scaling Up

Before you preheat your oven, you need to adjust your mindset. Baking two loaves is physically different from mixing dough for twenty people. When scaling up, pay close attention to your hydration levels. Larger batches of dough can feel significantly stickier and warmer because the mass retains heat generated during fermentation.

Mastering Sourdough Recipes for a Crowd

When selecting sourdough recipes for a crowd, you want items that offer high impact but low last-minute labor. You do not want to be shaping individual rolls while your guests are arriving. Instead, focus on large slab bakes or tear-and-share breads that can be prepared in advance and finished with a quick reheat.

A fantastic option is a massive Rosemary and Sea Salt Focaccia. This Italian classic allows you to feed a dozen people with a single pan. It utilizes a high-hydration dough which is easier to handle in large quantities than low-hydration sandwich breads. Because focaccia relies on olive oil for flavor and texture rather than perfect shaping, it is forgiving for beginners. You can let it cold-proof in the fridge overnight, which actually improves the flavor profile. The morning of your party, simply dimple the dough, add your toppings, and bake.

The Ultimate Sourdough Bruschetta Bar

For a more interactive dining experience, consider setting up a bruschetta bar. This allows your guests to customize their meal, reducing the pressure on you to serve a hot main dish immediately. The foundation of this bar is, of course, the bread.

You will want to bake two or three large batard loaves a day ahead. Stale sourdough actually holds up better to toppings than fresh bread because it won't get soggy as quickly. On the day of the party, slice the loaves into thick crostini, brush them with olive oil, and toast or grill them until golden brown.

Set out bowls of toppings such as:

  • Classic basil and heirloom tomato compote
  • Whipped ricotta with honey and cracked black pepper
  • Caramelized onions and balsamic glaze
  • Olive tapenade and fresh mozzarella

FAQ

Can I make sourdough dough ahead of time and freeze it?

Yes, freezing sourdough dough is a great strategy for parties. You can bulk ferment the dough, divide it into individual portions, and then freeze them in sealed bags. Let them thaw in the fridge overnight, then proceed with the final proof and baking the morning of your event.

How much starter do I need for a large batch?

Most recipes call for about 10% to 20% starter relative to the flour weight. For a large batch, you may need a full cup or more of active starter. Feed your starter 8 to 12 hours before you plan to mix to ensure it is at peak activity and bubbly.

Why does my large batch of dough ferment faster than usual?

Larger masses of dough generate their own heat during fermentation. This thermal mass can speed up the process significantly compared to a single small loaf. To slow it down, you can use colder water or autolyse (mix flour and water before adding starter and salt) to keep the dough temperature lower.
