1. Classic Holiday Sourdough Bread

This is the base recipe you’ll use for most of the festive loaves in this guide. It’s a straightforward 24‑hour schedule that yields a golden crust, a tender interior, and a mild tang that pairs perfectly with butter, cheese, or jam.

  1. Mix: In a large bowl combine 500 g bread flour, 350 g water, 100 g active sourdough starter, and 10 g salt. Stir until no dry spots remain.
  2. Autolyse: Let the dough rest 30 minutes. This hydrates the flour and improves gluten development.
  3. Fold: Using a Sourdough Whisk Set (the Danish dough whisk works wonders), perform a series of stretch‑and‑folds every 30 minutes for the next 2 hours.
  4. Bulk Ferment: Cover and let the dough rise at room temperature (around 70°F) until it has increased by about 30 %—usually 4‑5 hours.
  5. Shape: Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface, shape into a boule, and place seam‑side up in a proofing basket.
  6. Cold Retard: Refrigerate for 12‑16 hours. This develops flavor and makes scoring easier.
  7. Bake: Preheat a Dutch oven to 475°F, transfer the chilled dough, score with the included lame, cover, and bake 20 minutes. Remove the lid and bake another 20‑25 minutes until deep brown.

The result is a loaf that holds up well for slicing, toasting, or serving alongside holiday soups.

2. Cranberry‑Orange Sourdough Loaf

Adding dried cranberries and orange zest turns the classic base into a bright, festive treat. The citrus cuts through the sour tang, while the berries add a subtle chew.

  • Follow the Classic Holiday Sourdough Bread recipe through the bulk ferment stage.
  • During the final fold, incorporate 1 cup dried cranberries (soaked in warm water for 10 minutes, then drained) and the zest of one large orange.
  • Proceed with shaping, cold retard, and baking as described above.

For an extra glossy finish, brush the loaf with a light egg wash before the final 20‑minute bake.

3. Spiced Sourdough Pull‑Apart Bread

This loaf is perfect for sharing at family gatherings because it can be broken into hand‑size pieces. Warm spices like nutmeg, cinnamon, and cardamom give it a holiday aroma that fills the house.

Ingredients (adds to the base recipe): 1 tsp ground cinnamon, ½ tsp ground nutmeg, ¼ tsp ground cardamom, and 2 Tbsp melted butter folded in after the first fold.

After the cold retard, score the top with a shallow lattice using the lame from the Sourdough Whisk Set. Bake as usual, then let cool on a wire rack. Serve warm with a pat of butter for the ultimate comfort bite.

4. Sourdough Panettone‑Style Holiday Bread

If you want something that looks as impressive as it tastes, try this panettone‑inspired loaf. It’s richer, with butter and eggs, but still benefits from the natural leavening of sourdough.

Key adjustments:

  • Increase hydration to 75 % (about 375 g water for 500 g flour).
  • Add 3 large eggs and 100 g softened butter after the autolyse.
  • Fold in 1 cup mixed candied citrus peel and ½ cup raisins during the final stretch‑and‑fold.

Shape the dough into a tall cylinder, place in a greased 9‑inch loaf pan, and let it rise until it reaches the top of the pan (about 2 hours). Bake at 350°F for 45‑50 minutes, covering the top with foil if it browns too quickly. The result is a soft, fragrant loaf that makes a gorgeous centerpiece.

Tips for Baking Sourdough During Cold Winter Months

Winter temperatures can slow fermentation, so plan accordingly:

  • Place the dough in a warm spot (near a turned‑off oven with the light on, or on top of a refrigerator) to keep bulk fermentation on schedule.
  • If you’re short on time, give the dough a short warm rise (30‑45 minutes at 80°F) before the cold retard.
  • Use the dough scraper from the Sourdough Whisk Set to gently lift sticky dough without tearing it.

Remember, sourdough is forgiving—if the dough looks a bit under‑proofed, a longer cold retard will still develop flavor without compromising structure.

FAQ

Can I use the Sourdough Whisk Set with a gluten‑free starter?

Yes. The Danish dough whisk works well with gluten‑free flours, and the scraper helps keep the bowl clean. Just be aware that gluten‑free doughs are often stickier, so the scraper may be your primary tool.

How long can I keep the holiday sourdough loaves fresh?

Store the cooled loaves wrapped in a clean kitchen towel or in a paper bag for up to three days. For longer storage, slice and freeze; toast directly from the freezer for best texture.

Do I need to feed my starter more often in winter?

In colder months the starter’s activity slows, so a weekly feed at room temperature (or a warm spot) is usually sufficient. If you notice a sluggish rise, give it a couple of extra feedings at 70°F before baking.
