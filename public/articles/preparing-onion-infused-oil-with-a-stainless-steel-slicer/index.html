Step‑by‑Step: Preparing Onion‑Infused Oil with a Stainless Steel Slicer

Follow these steps for a batch that is ready to use in under an hour.

1. Peel and Stabilize the Onion
Cut off the root end, leaving a small portion to keep the layers together. Slice off the top, then make a shallow cut through the outer skin to remove it. Place the onion on the stainless steel slicer’s cutting board and insert the prongs of the Onion Holder & Slicing Guide into the root side. The prongs hold the onion steady while you glide the blade across.

2. Slice Evenly
Using the stainless steel slicer, cut the onion into 1‑mm‑thick rings. Because the slicer’s blade is sharp and the holder keeps the onion from moving, you’ll get uniform slices without tearing.

3. Lightly Cook the Slices
Heat a small saucepan over low heat and add the oil. When the oil glistens (about 40 °F), add the onion rings. Stir gently with a wooden spoon. The goal is to warm the oil enough to draw out flavor—do not brown the onions. Cooking for 5‑7 minutes is sufficient; the onions should become translucent and fragrant.

4. Cool and Strain
Remove the pan from heat and let the mixture cool to room temperature (about 20‑30 minutes). Place a fine‑mesh strainer over your glass jar and pour the oil through, leaving the onion pieces behind. If you prefer a stronger flavor, you can let the onions steep in the oil for an additional 30 minutes before straining.

5. Store Properly
Seal the jar tightly and store the oil in the refrigerator. Use within two weeks for optimal freshness. The oil can also be used cold in vinaigrettes, as a finishing drizzle, or as a base for sautéing vegetables.

Tips for Maximizing Flavor and Safety

Even with a reliable tool like the Onion Holder & Slicing Guide, a few tricks can elevate your oil:

  • Choose the right onion: Sweet onions (Vidalia, Walla Walla) yield a milder oil, while yellow onions give a deeper, caramel‑like note.
  • Control temperature: Keep the oil below 150 °F to avoid cooking the onions too much; you want extraction, not caramelization.
  • Use fresh oil: Old or rancid oil will mask the delicate onion aroma.
  • Clean the slicer promptly: Rinse the stainless steel slicer and the holder under warm water right after use to prevent onion residue from drying and sticking.

These practices keep the process safe, efficient, and flavorful every time.

FAQ

Can I use the stainless steel slicer for other vegetables?

Absolutely. The slicer’s sharp blade and the pronged holder work well with tomatoes, potatoes, and even firm fruits like apples. Just adjust the thickness setting if your slicer offers multiple cuts.

Is it safe to store onion‑infused oil at room temperature?

For short periods (up to 24 hours) it’s fine, but for longer storage refrigeration is recommended to prevent oxidation and maintain flavor.
