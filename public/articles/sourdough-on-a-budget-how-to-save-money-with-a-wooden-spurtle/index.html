Budget-Friendly Starter Maintenance Tips

Beyond the tools, how you manage your starter dictates your budget. If you keep your starter on the counter, you're feeding it daily, which consumes a significant amount of flour. To keep your sourdough on a budget, move your starter to the refrigerator.

Cold fermentation slows the yeast, meaning you only need to feed it once a week. When you do pull it out to feed, use your skinny spurtle to ensure all the old starter is fully incorporated with the new flour. This prevents "layering," where old starter sits at the bottom and goes unused, forcing you to discard more than necessary.

Cleaning and Caring for Your Wooden Tools

To ensure your investment lasts, avoid putting your wooden tools in the dishwasher. The high heat and harsh detergents can crack the wood and strip the natural oils.

Simply rinse your spurtle and dough stick with warm, soapy water immediately after use. For deeper cleaning, a quick scrub with a brush and a wipe of food-grade mineral oil once a month will keep the wood from absorbing odors and prevent it from becoming brittle. A well-maintained tool is a budget-friendly tool because you never have to buy a replacement.

FAQ

Can I use a regular spoon instead of a spurtle?

You can, but a regular spoon is usually too wide to reach the corners of a jar and too blunt to effectively blend thick dough. A spurtle's skinny profile reduces waste and makes the stirring process much faster.

Does the material of the stirrer affect the sourdough?

While the yeast doesn't "care" what you stir it with, wood is preferred because it is non-reactive. Some metals can react with the acidity of the sourdough over long periods, and some plastics can scratch your glass jars.

How often should I replace my wooden dough tools?

If cared for properly (hand-washed and occasionally oiled), a wooden spurtle can last a lifetime. You only need to replace it if the wood becomes deeply cracked or splintered.
