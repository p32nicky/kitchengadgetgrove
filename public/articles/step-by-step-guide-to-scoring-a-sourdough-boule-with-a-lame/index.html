Scoring a sourdough boule with lame is the moment that turns a humble dough into a bakery‑level loaf. A clean, confident cut lets steam escape, creates that coveted open crumb, and gives the crust an eye‑catching pattern. Why Scoring Matters (and How It Works)

When the oven’s heat hits the dough, the interior wants to expand. If the surface is sealed, the bread can burst unevenly, leading to a dense crumb or a cracked crust. Scoring provides a controlled outlet for the gas, directing the expansion along the lines you cut. A well‑scored boule also showcases your personal style, turning every loaf into a signature piece.

Preparing Your Dough for Scoring

Before you even pick up the lame, make sure the dough is ready:

  1. Final Proof. Your boule should have risen to about 85‑90% of its expected size. Over‑proofed dough will deflate when cut; under‑proofed dough won’t open fully.
  2. Surface Tension. Gently flip the boule onto a parchment sheet or a well‑floured proofing basket. Lightly mist the surface with water to keep it from drying out.
  3. Pre‑heat the Oven. Aim for 475°F (245°C) with a Dutch oven or baking stone inside. A hot oven creates the steam burst needed for a dramatic oven spring.

While the dough rests for a few minutes, you can give the lame a quick wipe with a damp cloth. This removes any metal shavings and ensures the blade is clean.

Step‑by‑Step Scoring Technique

Now for the main event. Follow these steps to score your sourdough boule with lame confidently:

  • Position the Lame. Hold the handle at a 30‑45° angle to the dough surface. The blade should be angled toward the center of the loaf.
  • Make a Quick, Decisive Cut. Apply firm, even pressure in a single motion. A hesitant, jagged slice will tear the crust and ruin the pattern.
  • Control the Depth. Aim for a cut about ¼ to ½ inch deep. Too shallow and the steam won’t escape; too deep and the loaf may lose structural integrity.
  • Pattern Ideas. Classic options include a single slash down the center, a cross, or a series of diagonal slashes. Whatever you choose, keep the lines clean and purposeful.

When you’re ready, slide the scored boule onto the preheated baking surface, cover it (if using a Dutch oven), and bake.

Tips for Consistent Results

Even with perfect technique, small variables can affect the outcome. Here are a few pro tips to keep your scores looking sharp:

  • Keep the Blade Sharp. Dull blades drag and crush the crust. Use a honing stone or a dedicated blade sharpener after every few uses.
  • Use Steam. If you don’t have a Dutch oven, toss a handful of ice cubes into the oven’s bottom tray right after loading the boule. The sudden steam helps the crust expand along the cuts.
  • Temperature Consistency. Avoid opening the oven door frequently. Each opening drops the temperature and can cause uneven oven spring.
  • Practice the Angle. The 30‑45° angle isn’t a rule set in stone; experiment with slight variations to find the line that gives you the most aesthetic rise.

Finishing Up and Storing Your Lame

After the bake, let the loaf cool on a wire rack for at least an hour before slicing. This rest period allows the crumb to set and the flavor to develop fully.

To keep your lame in top condition:

  1. Wipe the blade with a dry cloth while it’s still warm.
  2. Store it in a protective sheath or a dedicated drawer compartment.
  3. Periodically check the blade for nicks and replace it if needed.

Proper maintenance not only extends the life of your tool but also ensures every future score is as clean as the first.

FAQ

Do I need a special blade for scoring sourdough?

A razor‑sharp, single‑edge blade is ideal. The lame in the Sourdough Whisk Set is designed specifically for scoring and can be swapped out when dull.

Can I score a baguette the same way?

Yes, but baguettes benefit from a longer, shallower cut (about ¼ inch deep) along the length of the loaf. Adjust the angle to about 30° for a clean slice.

How often should I sharpen my lame?

Whenever you notice the blade dragging or producing ragged cuts—usually after 5‑7 breads. A quick honing with a fine stone restores the edge.
