Preparing Your Herbs for the Dough

Before you reach for your spurtle, consider how you prep your herbs. Fresh herbs provide a bright, floral note, while dried herbs offer a more concentrated, earthy punch. If using fresh herbs, pat them dry with a paper towel; excess moisture can create slippery pockets in your dough that disrupt the gluten structure.

For a more intense flavor, try infusing your starter. When feeding your starter, use the skinny wooden spurtle spatula to stir in finely minced herbs. Because the spurtle is narrow and firm, it can scrape the corners of your glass jar, ensuring the herbs are blended into the starter rather than sticking to the glass walls.

Using Your Herb Sourdough Wooden Spurtle Technique

Once your bulk fermentation is underway, it is time to add your flavorings. I recommend adding herbs during the first set of stretch-and-folds. This allows the botanicals to be woven into the gluten network without over-handling the dough.

Here is my preferred method for a seamless blend:

  • The Sprinkle: Dust your herbs evenly across the surface of the dough.
  • The Slice: Use the skinny wooden spurtle to gently "cut" into the dough, pushing the herbs down into the center.
  • The Fold: Grasp the edge of the dough and fold it over the herbs.
  • The Scrape: Use the spurtle to scrape any stray herbs or sticking dough from the sides of the bowl, folding them back into the center.

Using the Danish-style dough stick from the set is excellent for the initial mixing phase, while the skinny spurtle handles the detailed work of incorporating the herbs and cleaning the jar.

Choosing the Right Herb Combinations

Not all herbs play well with the tang of sourdough. Since sourdough has a natural acidity, you want herbs that can stand up to that profile. Rosemary and thyme are classics for sourdough because their woody notes complement the fermented flavor.

For a more Mediterranean vibe, try a blend of dried oregano, basil, and a pinch of garlic powder. If you are feeling adventurous, fresh dill and caraway seeds create a wonderful rye-adjacent flavor profile. Cleaning Up Your Baking Station

One of the most frustrating parts of sourdough is the "sticky residue" left in the jars. When you're working with herbs, this is even worse because small pieces of greenery tend to cling to the sides. This is where the skinny spurtle truly shines.

Because of its slender profile, the spurtle acts as a precision scraper. You can glide it along the curve of your jar to collect every last bit of herb-infused starter. FAQ

Can I add dried herbs directly to the starter?

Yes, you can! Adding herbs during the feeding stage allows the flavors to meld. Use the skinny spurtle to ensure the dried herbs are fully submerged and not just floating on the surface.

Why use a wooden spurtle instead of a silicone spatula?

Wooden spurtles provide a firmer structure that is better for "cutting" through thick sourdough. Additionally, the specific shape of the sourdough spurtle is designed to reach the bottom of narrow jars more effectively than a wide silicone head.

Will adding herbs affect the rise of my bread?

Generally, no. As long as you aren't adding massive amounts of salt or oil along with your herbs, your wild yeast should be unaffected. The key is to fold them in gently using a tool like the Danish dough whisk or spurtle to avoid degassing the dough.
