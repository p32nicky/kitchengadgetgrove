The Secret to Perfect Campfire Corn and Sausage

The biggest challenge with campfire cooking is heat management. Unlike a kitchen stove, a grill or fire pit has hot spots and cool zones. To ensure your corn doesn't burn while your sausages are still raw in the middle, I recommend the "staggered start" method.

Corn takes longer to cook through to the cob, so get it on the grate first. Sausages, depending on the thickness, usually need about 12-15 minutes. If you're using fresh corn in the husk, it can stay on longer without scorching. If you've shucked it, keep a close eye on it and rotate frequently.

Three Easy Grilled Corn and Sausage Recipes for Camping

Depending on the vibe of your trip, you can take these ingredients in a few different directions. Here are my three go-to combinations:

  • The Classic Americana: Brush your corn with melted butter and salt. Pair it with juicy bratwursts. Once off the grill, top the corn with a squeeze of lime and the sausages with spicy brown mustard.
  • The Southwest Sizzle: Rub your corn with a mix of chili powder and cumin. Use spicy chorizo or Andouille sausage. Serve with a dollop of sour cream or crumbled cotija cheese for a street-corn inspired feast.
  • The Garlic-Herb Feast: Mix melted butter with minced garlic and dried parsley. Use smoked kielbasa or Italian sausages. This combination is great for those who want a more savory, aromatic meal.

Mastering the Grill: Step-by-Step Guide

To put these grilled corn and sausage recipes for camping into action, follow this workflow to keep your dinner synchronized:

First, prep your corn. If you're shucking them, lightly coat them in oil or butter to prevent sticking. Place them on the outer edges of the grill where the heat is more moderate. In the center, place your sausages. This allows the sausages to sear quickly while the corn roasts slowly.

Every five minutes, rotate the corn a quarter turn. When the kernels start to turn golden-brown with a few charred black spots, they are ready. At the same time, your sausages should be plump and browned. FAQ

Should I grill corn in the husk or shucked?

Both work! Grilling in the husk steams the corn, resulting in a juicier kernel. Shucking the corn first allows you to get those direct char marks and apply seasonings directly to the kernels. If you shuck them, definitely use holders to keep your hands clean.

What's the best sausage for camping?

Pre-cooked smoked sausages (like kielbasa) are the easiest because you are essentially just reheating and browning them. For a more gourmet experience, raw bratwursts or Italian sausages are great, but they require a slower cook time to ensure they are safe to eat.

How do I prevent my corn from sticking to the grill?

The best way to prevent sticking is to ensure your grill grates are clean and preheated. Lightly brushing the corn with an oil with a high smoke point (like avocado or canola oil) or melted butter creates a barrier between the vegetable and the metal.
