2. Building Your Starter: The Foundation of Every Loaf

Before you even touch flour, you need a healthy starter. Here’s a quick routine:

  1. Mix equal parts flour (whole wheat or rye for extra flavor) and water in a jar.
  2. Cover loosely and let sit at room temperature for 24 hours.
  3. Discard half, feed with fresh flour and water, and repeat daily.
  4. After 5–7 days, your starter should double in size, be bubbly, and have a pleasant tang.

Keep the starter in a small, reusable container—your 3‑in‑1 Sourdough Whisk Set’s whisk and scraper are perfect for feeding and mixing without cross‑contamination.

3. From Dough to Dough: Mixing and Autolyse

Mixing is where the gluten network starts to form. The Danish dough whisk in the starter kit is designed to incorporate air without overworking the dough. Use a light touch: mix just until the flour is absorbed, then let the dough rest (autolyse) for 30–60 minutes. This rest period improves extensibility and flavor.

4. Shaping, Proofing, and Scoring the Masterpiece

Once your bulk fermentation is complete, divide, shape, and proof:

  • Shape: Use the whisk to gently fold the dough into a round or batard, then tighten the surface with the scraper for a smooth finish.
  • Proof: Let the shaped dough rise in a banneton or bowl lined with a well‑floured towel for 2–3 hours at room temperature, then chill overnight for a slow, flavor‑developing rise.
  • Score: Just before baking, use the bread scoring lame to cut a few shallow slashes. The lame’s fine edge gives precise, clean cuts that allow the loaf to expand beautifully.

Preheat your oven to 500°F (260°C) with a Dutch oven inside. Once the dough is scored, slide it into the preheated pot, cover, and bake for 20 minutes. Remove the lid, lower the temperature to 450°F (232°C), and bake another 25–30 minutes until the crust is deep amber.

5. Troubleshooting Common Issues

Even with a solid course, real‑world baking throws curveballs. Here are quick fixes:

  • Dough too sticky: Add a tablespoon of flour per 100 ml of water during the initial mix.
  • Crust too pale: Increase oven temperature by 25°F or add a splash of water to the baking stone for steam.
  • Crumb too dense: Extend the bulk fermentation by an hour or two; more time means more gas retention.

Use the scraper to gently fold the dough during bulk fermentation to encourage bubble formation without overmixing.

FAQ

What type of flour should I use for a starter?

Whole wheat or rye gives a faster rise and richer flavor. Once your starter is mature, you can switch to all‑purpose flour for the final dough.

How long does a sourdough loaf take to bake?

From shaping to finished loaf, expect about 50–60 minutes in a preheated Dutch oven. The first 20 minutes are at a higher temperature to create steam.

Can I bake sourdough in a regular oven?

Yes, but the crust will be less crisp. Place a pan of water in the bottom of the oven to generate steam, and consider using a baking stone for better heat distribution.
