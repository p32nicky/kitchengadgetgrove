Why Sourdough Ciabatta Beats Store‑Bought Bread

Commercial ciabatta often relies on additives to keep it soft and extend shelf life. A true sourdough version brings natural fermentation, which develops flavor, improves digestibility, and creates a crust that stays firm longer. The open crumb also traps air, making each bite light and airy – ideal for stacking turkey, roasted veggies, or a classic Caprese.

Ingredients You’ll Need

  • 500 g (about 4 cups) bread flour
  • 380 ml (1 ½ cups) water, lukewarm
  • 100 g active sourdough starter (100 % hydration)
  • 10 g (2 tsp) fine sea salt
  • Optional: 1 tbsp olive oil for a richer crumb

All measurements are weight‑based for consistency. If you prefer volume, the flour and water amounts convert to roughly 4 cups and 1½ cups respectively, but a kitchen scale will give the best results.

Step‑by‑Step: From Starter to Sandwich‑Ready Loaf

1. Autolyse (30 minutes) – In a large bowl, combine the flour and water. Stir with the Danish dough whisk from the 3‑in‑1 Sourdough Whisk Set until just incorporated. Cover and let sit. This hydrates the flour, making the gluten easier to develop later.

2. Add starter and salt – Sprinkle the active starter over the autolysed dough, then add the salt. Using the whisk, fold the starter in until the dough looks cohesive. The dough will be sticky – that’s normal.

3. Bulk fermentation (4‑5 hours) – Let the dough rest at room temperature. Every 30 minutes for the first 2 hours, perform a set of stretch‑and‑folds: pull a portion of dough upward, then fold it over the rest. The dough should become smoother and slightly less tacky.

4. Shaping – Lightly flour a work surface. With the dough scraper from the whisk set, turn the dough out and gently shape it into a rough rectangle, being careful not to deflate the airy interior. Fold the short ends toward the center, then flip the dough so the seam is on the bottom. Let it rest 15 minutes, then shape into a classic ciabatta loaf by folding the dough over itself once more.

5. Proof (90 minutes) – Transfer the shaped loaf onto a well‑floured parchment sheet. Cover loosely with a damp towel and let it rise until it looks puffy and has increased by about 30 %.

6. Scoring – Preheat your oven to 475°F (245°C) with a Dutch oven or a baking stone inside. Just before baking, use the bread scoring lame from the whisk set to make a shallow slash across the top of the loaf. This directs steam expansion and creates the classic ciabatta “slit.”

7. Bake – Slide the parchment onto the hot stone or place the loaf inside the Dutch oven, cover, and bake for 20 minutes. Remove the cover and bake another 10‑15 minutes until the crust is deep golden and the internal temperature hits 205°F (96°C).

Let the loaf cool on a rack for at least 30 minutes before slicing. This rest lets the crumb set, ensuring clean slices for your sandwiches.

Tips for Perfect Sandwiches Every Time

• Slice while slightly warm. A serrated knife will give clean cuts without squashing the airy crumb.

• Toast lightly. A quick toast in a skillet with a drizzle of olive oil adds crunch and helps prevent soggy fillings.

• Store properly. Wrap the cooled loaf in a linen towel and place it in a paper bag. For longer storage, freeze slices individually and re‑toast when needed.

These small steps keep your ciabatta fresh and ready for any lunch or dinner sandwich.

FAQ

Can I use all‑purpose flour instead of bread flour?

Yes, but the loaf will have a slightly softer crust and a less pronounced chew. Bread flour’s higher protein content helps develop stronger gluten, which is ideal for the open crumb of ciabatta.

Do I need to refrigerate the dough after shaping?

Refrigeration isn’t required for this recipe, but a cold retard (8‑12 hours in the fridge) can deepen flavor and make the dough easier to handle. If you choose to retard, reduce the final proof time accordingly.

How do I know when the dough is fully fermented?

The dough should feel airy and pass the “poke test”: gently press a fingertip into the dough; if the indentation slowly springs back, the fermentation is complete and the loaf is ready to bake.
