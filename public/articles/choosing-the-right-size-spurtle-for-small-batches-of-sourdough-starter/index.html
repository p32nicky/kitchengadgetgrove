Why the Size of Your Spurtle Matters

A spurtle might look simple, but its dimensions affect three key tasks: mixing, scraping, and aerating. If the stick is too short, you’ll spend extra time lifting the jar to reach the bottom, which can introduce unwanted heat and reduce the starter’s activity. Too long, and you risk breaking the wooden handle or losing control when stirring a small volume. The right length gives you full reach while keeping the grip comfortable, allowing you to incorporate air evenly—a critical step for a healthy starter.

Common Spurtle Lengths and Their Ideal Uses

Most wooden spurtles range from 6 to 12 inches. Here’s a quick rundown:

  1. 6‑inch spurtle: Best for tiny jars (4‑oz to 8‑oz) and for stirring directly in a mixing bowl. Its short profile gives excellent control but may not reach the bottom of larger containers.
  2. 8‑inch spurtle: The sweet spot for most home bakers. It comfortably reaches the bottom of 16‑oz to 32‑oz jars, making it ideal for small batches of starter and for scraping the last bits of culture from the sides.
  3. 10‑12 inch spurtle: Designed for large fermentation vessels (quart‑size or bigger). It can be unwieldy for a ½‑cup starter, and the extra length can cause splashing.

For the typical home sourdough enthusiast who keeps starter in a 16‑oz glass jar, an 8‑inch spurtle provides the best balance of reach and maneuverability.

How to Test a Spurtle Before You Commit

Before you decide on a size, try a quick hands‑on test:

  • Fill a 16‑oz jar half full with water.
  • Insert the spurtle; it should reach the bottom without the handle touching the water’s surface.
  • Rotate the stick. If you feel resistance near the top, the spurtle may be too long for precise work.

If the spurtle meets these criteria, it’s likely a good fit for a small starter batch. The 2‑piece set includes an 8‑inch stick and a slightly shorter 6‑inch version, giving you flexibility for different jar sizes.

Practical Tips for Using Your Spurtle with Small Batches

When you’re feeding a starter that’s only a few ounces, follow these steps for consistent results:

  1. Warm the starter: Let the jar sit at room temperature for 30 minutes. Warm starter is easier to stir.
  2. Use the skinny wooden spurtle: The tapered tip penetrates the thick layer of starter quickly, reducing the effort needed to break up clumps.
  3. Scrape the sides: After mixing, run the spurtle along the glass to collect any leftover culture. This ensures you’re not discarding viable yeast.
  4. Seal and store: A snug lid prevents contamination, and the wooden handle won’t leave marks on the glass.

These habits keep your starter lively and your jars clean—a win‑win for any home baker.

Cleaning and Maintaining Your Wooden Spurtle

Proper care prolongs the life of your spurtle set. After each use, rinse the stick with warm water—no soap needed, as it can soak into the wood. FAQ

Do I need a different spurtle for each starter batch size?

No. One well‑chosen spurtle—typically the 8‑inch length—covers most small‑batch scenarios. The set’s 6‑inch stick offers extra precision for very tiny jars.

Can I use a metal spoon instead of a wooden spurtle?

While metal spoons work, they can scratch glass and may react with the acidic starter. Wood is non‑reactive and gentle on jars, making it the preferred material.

Is the wooden spurtle safe for high‑heat applications?

The spurtle is designed for mixing and scraping at room temperature. It’s not intended for direct contact with hot surfaces, but it can safely stir warm starter (under 120°F) without damage.
