What a Wooden Spurtle Is and How It Handles Sourdough

A spurtle is a thin, tapered wooden stick traditionally used in Scottish cooking to stir porridge. In the sourdough world, the wooden spurtle’s narrow profile makes it ideal for stirring a starter that lives in a tall jar. Because wood is non‑reactive, it won’t alter the starter’s acidity, and the natural grain provides a gentle grip that scrapes the sides without tearing the delicate gluten network.

What a Dough Scraper Does Differently

A dough scraper (sometimes called a bench scraper) is typically a flat, stainless‑steel blade with a wooden or silicone handle. It excels at lifting, folding, and cutting dough on a work surface. For sourdough, the scraper’s broad edge can quickly scoop a thick starter out of a bowl, but the metal surface can be harsh on the starter’s thin membrane if you’re not careful.

Key Comparison Points

  • Material interaction: Wood is inert and won’t react with the starter’s acids; steel is safe but can scratch glass jars.
  • Shape: The spurtle’s tapered shape reaches the bottom of narrow containers, while the scraper’s flat edge works best on wide surfaces.
  • Cleaning: Both tools are easy to rinse, but the wooden spurtle dries quickly and resists rust.
  • Multi‑purpose use: The spurtle doubles as a jar‑scraper, dough‑stirrer, and even a light whisk; the scraper is primarily a cutting and lifting tool.

When a Dough Scraper Is More Practical

During the bulk fermentation stage, you’ll be handling a sticky, elastic dough that needs to be folded and turned several times. A stainless‑steel dough scraper provides the leverage to lift the dough without tearing it, especially when dusted with flour. It also helps you clean the work surface quickly, preventing dough from sticking to countertops—something a narrow spurtle can’t accomplish as efficiently.

Choosing the Right Tool for Your Routine

Most sourdough bakers end up using both tools at different stages. Here’s a simple workflow to decide which to reach for:

  1. Feeding the starter: Use the wooden spurtle to stir the starter, scrape the sides, and ensure an even distribution of flour and water.
  2. Transferring to a bowl: If the starter is thick, a dough scraper can scoop it out cleanly.
  3. Mixing the final dough: The spurtle’s thin profile can whisk together water and flour before you turn the dough onto the bench.
  4. Bench work: Switch to a dough scraper for folding, cutting, and cleaning the surface.

By keeping the 2 PCS Wooden Spurtle Spatula set on hand, you eliminate the need for a separate whisk or jar‑scraper, and the sturdy wooden handle feels comfortable even after 30 minutes of vigorous mixing.

Care Tips for Longevity

Both tools are low‑maintenance, but a few habits will keep them performing:

  • Rinse the wooden spurtle immediately after use and dry it on a towel; avoid soaking it for long periods.
  • Season the wood occasionally with a light coat of food‑grade mineral oil to prevent cracking.
  • Wash the steel scraper with warm, soapy water and dry it thoroughly to avoid rust spots.

With proper care, the wooden spurtle can last for years, becoming a familiar part of your sourdough routine.

FAQ

Can I use a wooden spurtle with a metal starter jar?

Yes. Wood will not react with metal, and the spurtle’s smooth surface won’t scratch the interior, making it safe for any jar material.

Is the wooden spurtle dishwasher‑safe?

It’s best to hand‑wash and dry it promptly. Prolonged exposure to dishwasher heat and water can cause wood to warp or split over time.

Do I need a separate dough scraper for shaping loaves?

If you frequently shape high‑hydration doughs, a stainless‑steel dough scraper is useful for lifting and turning the dough on a floured surface. The wooden spurtle can assist with mixing but isn’t a substitute for the scraper’s leverage.
