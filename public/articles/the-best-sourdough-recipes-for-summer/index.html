1. Classic Summer Sourdough Loaf

This loaf is the foundation for any summer sandwich or bruschetta. The hydration is a touch higher than a typical winter loaf, which yields a tender crumb and a crisp crust that holds up well with fresh toppings.

  • 350 g bread flour
  • 50 g whole‑wheat flour
  • 350 ml cold water
  • 10 g sea salt

Mix the flours and water in a bowl, cover, and let autolyse for 30 minutes. 2. Lemon‑Herb Sourdough Baguettes

Bright citrus and fresh herbs turn ordinary baguettes into a summer side dish. The dough’s higher hydration (78%) keeps the crumb airy, while a quick 30‑minute proof in a cool kitchen prevents over‑proofing.

  1. Combine 300 g sourdough starter, 400 g bread flour, 300 ml ice‑cold water, and 10 g salt.
  2. Fold in zest of two lemons and 2 tablespoons chopped fresh thyme.
  3. Use the dough scraper from the Sourdough Whisk Set to turn the dough during the bulk rise—its flat edge helps keep the surface clean.
  4. Shape into baguettes, place on a parchment‑lined sheet, cover, and let rise 30 minutes.
  5. Score with the lame, steam the oven, and bake at 475°F for 15‑18 minutes.

The result is a fragrant loaf with a golden crust that pairs beautifully with grilled fish or a summer salad.

3. Summer Fruit Sourdough Muffins

These muffin‑style breads are perfect for a quick breakfast or a snack on the go. By using a stiff sourdough starter and folding in fresh berries at the end, you get a tender interior with a slightly tangy edge.

  • 200 g active starter
  • 250 g all‑purpose flour
  • 150 ml milk, chilled
  • 50 g melted butter
  • 2 eggs
  • 60 g honey
  • 1 tsp baking soda
  • 200 g mixed summer berries (blueberries, raspberries, blackberries)

Whisk the wet ingredients together with the Danish dough whisk, then fold in the dry ingredients. Gently fold in the berries using the dough scraper to avoid crushing them. Spoon the batter into a greased muffin tin and bake at 375°F for 18‑20 minutes. The muffins stay moist for days, making them ideal for picnics.

4. Grilled Sourdough Pizza with Fresh Tomato & Basil

Grilling gives pizza a smoky flavor that’s perfect for summer evenings. Start with a thin sourdough crust, then top with ripe tomatoes, fresh basil, and a drizzle of olive oil.

  1. Prepare a basic sourdough pizza dough (250 g starter, 300 g bread flour, 180 ml water, 8 g salt).
  2. After the first rise, divide the dough and stretch each piece on a lightly floured surface.
  3. Pre‑heat a grill to medium‑high heat and oil the grates.
  4. Place the stretched dough directly on the grill, cover, and cook 2‑3 minutes per side until charred.
  5. Remove, add sliced tomatoes, torn basil leaves, and a sprinkle of mozzarella. Return to the grill for another minute to melt the cheese.

Use the lame to score a few shallow lines on the crust before grilling; the cuts help steam escape and keep the base crisp.

5. Sourdough Summer Salad Bowl with Warm Croutons

Turn stale sourdough into crunchy croutons that add texture to any salad. Cube the leftover loaf, toss with olive oil, salt, and a pinch of garlic powder, then toast in a 350°F oven for 12‑15 minutes.

While the croutons bake, whisk together a vinaigrette using the Danish dough whisk: 3 tablespoons apple cider vinegar, 1 ½ cups extra‑virgin olive oil, 1 tsp honey, and a splash of Dijon mustard. Toss mixed greens, sliced strawberries, cucumber, and feta cheese with the vinaigrette, then top with warm sourdought croutons for a refreshing summer bowl.

FAQ

Can I use the 3-in-1 Sourdough Whisk Set for gluten‑free sourdough?

Yes. The whisk’s flexible wires and the scraper’s flat edge work equally well with gluten‑free flours. Just handle the dough gently, as gluten‑free mixtures are more delicate.

How do I keep my sourdough starter from overheating in summer?

Store the starter in a cool spot—like a pantry or a basement—and feed it twice a day with equal parts flour and water. If temperatures exceed 80°F, you can place the starter in a refrigerator for a short 12‑hour rest between feedings.

Do I need to adjust hydration for hot weather?

Increasing hydration by 5‑10% helps the dough stay supple when the kitchen is warm. The recipes above already incorporate slightly higher hydration to accommodate summer temperatures.
