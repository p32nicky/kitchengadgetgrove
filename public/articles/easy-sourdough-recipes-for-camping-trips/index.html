When you’re out on a trail, the idea of pulling a warm, crusty loaf out of a campfire is pure magic. The right sourdough recipes for camping can be made with just a few basic tools, a portable starter, and a little patience. Below are practical recipes and tips that work whether you’re sleeping in a tent, a hammock, or a tiny RV.

Choosing the Right Sourdough Starter for the Trail

A healthy starter is the backbone of every sourdough project, and on a camping trip you need one that stays active without a refrigerator. Start with a dry starter or a rehydrated starter that you’ve fed the night before. Pack the starter in a sealed, lightweight container (a small mason jar works well) and keep it in a cool, dark spot of your pack. Feed it with equal parts water and flour every 12 hours while you’re on the trail; the extra activity keeps the microbes vigorous and the flavor bright.

Campfire Sourdough “Bannock”

This quick flatbread is the most forgiving recipe for beginners and works on any open flame. You’ll need:

  • 1 cup active sourdough starter
  • 1 cup all‑purpose flour
  • ½ cup water (adjust for consistency)
  • ¼ teaspoon salt
  • Optional: herbs, cheese, or honey for flavor

1. In a lightweight bowl, combine starter, flour, water, and salt. Use the Danish dough whisk to blend until a shaggy dough forms. 2. Let the dough rest for 30 minutes; this autolyse step reduces kneading. 3. Lightly flour a clean surface and turn the dough using the scraper; knead for about 1 minute until smooth. 4. Shape the dough into a ½‑inch thick disc and place it on a greased cast‑iron skillet over the campfire. 5. Cook 4–5 minutes per side, or until golden brown. Serve warm with butter or jam.

Portable Sourdough Loaf in a Dutch Oven

For a more traditional loaf, a small Dutch oven (2‑quart) fits into most camp stoves. Ingredients:

  • 200 g (about 1 ½ cups) active starter
  • 350 g (≈ 2 ¾ cups) bread flour
  • 150 g (≈ ⅔ cup) water
  • 7 g (1 ½ tsp) salt

1. Mix starter, flour, and water with the whisk until no dry spots remain. Cover and let sit 30 minutes. 2. Add salt, then knead on a lightly floured board using the scraper for about 3 minutes—don’t overwork; camp dough benefits from a bit of slack. 3. Shape into a ball, place in a lightly oiled Dutch oven, and cover. 4. Build a moderate fire (around 350 °F) and place the pot on a grill grate. Cook 30 minutes, then remove the lid and cook another 10‑15 minutes for a crisp crust. 5. Use the bread‑scoring lame to slash the top before the final uncovered bake; the cuts allow steam to escape and create an attractive pattern.

Tips for Managing Dough in Variable Weather

Temperature swings can surprise even seasoned bakers. Here are three quick adjustments:

  1. Cold mornings (below 50 °F): Increase the starter’s activity by feeding it with warm water and letting it sit in your pack’s insulated compartment for an extra hour before mixing.
  2. Hot afternoons (above 80 °F): Reduce the fermentation time by 20‑30 % and keep the dough covered with a damp cloth to prevent the crust from forming too quickly.
  3. High humidity: Add a tablespoon extra flour to the dough to maintain a workable consistency; the scraper will help you keep the surface clean.

These tweaks keep your sourdough recipes for camping on track, no matter the conditions.

Storing Leftover Bread on the Trail

Fresh sourdough stays good for several days, but you don’t want to waste any on a multi‑day trek. Wrap cooled loaves in a clean, breathable cloth (a tea towel works) and place them in a resealable bag. The bag protects against insects while allowing enough airflow to keep the crust crisp. If you have a portable freeze‑dry kit, you can even dehydrate slices for a lightweight snack.

FAQ

Can I use whole‑wheat flour for camp sourdough?

Yes. Whole‑wheat flour adds flavor but absorbs more water, so increase the hydration by about 10 %. The dough may be denser, so a longer autolyse (45 minutes) helps develop gluten.

Is the 3-in-1 Sourdough Whisk Set dishwasher safe?

The stainless‑steel components are dishwasher safe, but rinsing immediately after use prevents stuck flour from hardening. A quick hand‑wash with warm, soapy water is often enough on the trail.

What if I don’t have a Dutch oven?

A heavy cast‑iron skillet with a lid works as a substitute. Place a layer of foil under the dough to protect the pan from direct flame, and follow the same timing guidelines.
