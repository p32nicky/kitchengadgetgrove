The Case for Metal Scrapers: Precision and Power

Metal dough scrapers, often called "bench scrapers," are the heavy lifters of the kitchen. They typically feature a stainless steel blade with a comfortable handle. When you're weighing metal vs wooden dough scraper sticky dough capabilities, metal wins for sheer efficiency in cleaning.

Because the edge of a metal scraper is thin and rigid, it can get underneath a sticky mass of dough and lift it clean off a granite or stainless steel surface. This is essential for "bench clearing." If you're working with a wet sourdough that clings to everything, a metal blade allows you to scrape the surface flush, ensuring no waste is left behind.

Metal tools are also superior for dividing dough. If you need to cut a large batch of rolls or slice a boule into equal portions, the sharp, straight edge of a metal scraper provides a clean cut without pulling or tearing the gluten structure.

The Case for Wooden Scrapers: Gentle Handling

Wooden scrapers (or dough bowls) are less about cleaning the counter and more about managing the dough itself. Wood has a natural property that many bakers love: dough tends to stick to it slightly less than it does to some plastics or metals during the folding process.

A wooden scraper is ideal for the "stretch and fold" phase of sourdough. Because the edges are rounded and blunt, you can dive into a sticky mass of dough and lift it without cutting through the delicate air bubbles you've spent hours creating. It acts more like an extension of your hand.

Wooden tools are also the safest choice for non-stick pans or delicate plastic tubs. If you're fermenting your dough in a plastic container, a metal blade will leave permanent scratches. Wood keeps your equipment pristine while still giving you a way to move sticky dough without getting your fingers completely coated in gluey batter.

Which One Wins for Sticky Dough?

The truth is, you shouldn't choose just one. For high-hydration doughs, you need a workflow that utilizes both. Here is how to use them in tandem:

  • The Wooden Tool: Use this for mixing, folding, and transferring the dough from the bowl to the counter. It handles the "sticky" part of the process without damaging the dough.
  • The Metal Tool: Use this once the dough is on the counter. Use it to divide the dough and, most importantly, to scrape the counter clean after you've finished shaping.

Maintaining Your Tools for Long-Term Use

To keep your scrapers performing well, maintenance differs by material. Metal scrapers are low-maintenance; a quick rinse with hot soapy water and a dry towel is usually enough. However, always dry them immediately to prevent any potential spotting or corrosion over several years.

Wooden tools require more love. Never put a wooden scraper in the dishwasher, as the high heat and chemicals will crack the wood and strip away its natural oils. Instead, hand wash them and occasionally rub them with a food-grade mineral oil. This creates a barrier that makes the wood even more resistant to sticking, which is a huge advantage when dealing with sticky dough.

FAQ

Will a metal scraper scratch my granite countertops?

Generally, stainless steel is softer than granite, but if you use a scraping motion with excessive force or at a very sharp angle, there is a slight risk of scratching. To be safe, keep the blade relatively flat against the surface.

Can I use a wooden scraper to divide dough?

You can, but it won't be a clean cut. Wooden scrapers are better for lifting and folding. For precise portions and clean edges, a metal bench scraper is the superior choice.

Why does my dough stick to the scraper?

Sticky dough is often a result of high hydration. To prevent sticking, lightly dust your scraper with flour or dip it in water before it touches the dough. For wooden tools, ensuring they are well-seasoned with mineral oil helps significantly.
