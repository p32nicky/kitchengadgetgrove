1. Under‑ or Over‑Hydrating the Dough

Hydration is the backbone of a good sourdough crumb. Too much water yields a sticky mess; too little makes a dense, dry loaf. The key is to measure flour and water by weight, not volume, and adjust based on the flour’s absorption.

2. Ignoring the Importance of Temperature

Both dough temperature and ambient kitchen temperature affect fermentation speed. A common mistake is to let the dough sit in a chilly kitchen, slowing the starter and leading to a bland flavor. Aim for a dough temperature of 78°F–82°F (25°C–28°C) after mixing.

Use a simple thermometer to monitor the dough. If it’s too cool, place the bowl in a turned‑off oven with the light on, or use a warm water bath. If it’s too warm, move the dough to a cooler spot or briefly refrigerate it.

3. Skipping the Autolyse Step

Autolyse—letting flour and water rest before adding salt and starter—improves gluten development and flavor. Skipping it often results in a dough that feels tough to knead and a crumb that’s uneven.

Mix flour and water, cover, and let sit for 20‑40 minutes. Then incorporate the starter and salt using the dough scraper from the 3‑in‑1 Sourdough Whisk Set. The scraper’s flexible edge helps fold the starter in without tearing the developing gluten network.

4. Over‑Proofing or Under‑Proofing the Loaf

Proofing is where the dough gains its airy structure. Over‑proofed dough collapses; under‑proofed dough bursts open with a tight crumb. The "poke test" is a reliable gauge: gently press the dough—if the indentation springs back slowly, it’s ready.Another tip is to keep a log of proof times at your typical room temperature. Adjust based on the starter’s activity level; a highly active starter may need less time.

5. Scoring Incorrectly

Scoring isn’t just decorative; it directs oven spring. A common mistake is using a dull knife or a regular kitchen blade, which can crush the dough instead of slicing cleanly. The bread scoring lame included in the Sourdough Whisk Set is razor‑sharp and designed for precise, confident cuts.

Practice a few shallow scores on a piece of parchment before tackling the final loaf. Aim for a depth of about ¼ inch and angle the lame at 45 degrees to the dough surface. This creates an attractive bloom and helps the loaf expand evenly.

6. Forgetting to Steam the Oven

Steam in the first 15 minutes of baking keeps the crust flexible, allowing the loaf to fully expand before forming a crust. Bakers often neglect this, ending up with a tight, pale crust. Place a metal tray on the bottom rack and add a cup of hot water just as you load the loaf, or toss a few ice cubes onto the tray.
