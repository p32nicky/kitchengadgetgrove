How to Make Mexican Trompo Al Pastor Without a Motor

There is nothing quite like the sight of a spinning trompo at a street corner in Mexico City, with the taquero expertly slicing thin ribbons of marinated pork onto a corn tortilla. For most of us, the barrier to recreating this at home is the equipment. We don't have a commercial vertical broiler or a motorized rotisserie.

The good news is that you can absolutely achieve that signature charred exterior and tender interior by making a trompo al pastor without motor. The secret lies in using a vertical roasting setup that allows the heat to circulate and the juices to baste the meat naturally as it cooks in your oven.

The Gear: How to Roast Vertically at Home

To get the right texture, you need the meat to stand upright. If you lay pork shoulder on a baking sheet, you're just making carnitas. To get the "trompo" (spinning top) effect, you need a dedicated vertical tool. I highly recommend using a Vertical Gyro Pan with Skewer for Oven.

Because it's made of stainless steel, it handles the high heat of the oven while the pan at the bottom catches all those spiced drippings, which you can later use to warm your tortillas or brush back onto the meat.

Prepping the Perfect Al Pastor Marinade

The flavor of al pastor comes from the marriage of achiote paste and dried chilies. To get that vibrant red color and deep earthy taste, blend the following ingredients into a smooth paste:

  • Achiote paste (the heart of the flavor)
  • Dried Guajillo chilies (soaked and deseeded)
  • Apple cider vinegar and pineapple juice (the acidity tenderizes the meat)
  • Garlic, cumin, oregano, and a pinch of cloves

Slice your pork shoulder into thin steaks, about a quarter-inch thick. Rub the marinade generously on both sides of every slice. For the best results, let the meat marinate in the fridge for at least 12 hours. This ensures the flavors penetrate deep into the muscle fibers, which is crucial when you're roasting a large stack of meat.

Assembling Your Trompo Al Pastor Without Motor

Since we aren't using a motor to spin the meat, the way you stack the pork determines how evenly it cooks. Start by placing your Vertical Gyro Pan with Skewer for Oven on a sturdy baking sheet for extra stability. Slide the pork slices onto the skewer one by one, pressing down firmly after every few layers to create a dense, compact cylinder.

The crowning touch is the pineapple. Cut a fresh pineapple into thick rings or a large wedge and secure it to the very top of the skewer. As the oven heats up, the sugars in the pineapple caramelize and drip down the sides of the pork, naturally basting the meat and adding that essential sweet-and-savory contrast.

Cooking and Slicing Techniques

Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). Place your assembled trompo inside and roast for about 2 to 3 hours, depending on the size of your meat stack. Since you are making a trompo al pastor without motor, you won't have a mechanical rotation. To compensate, I suggest rotating the entire pan 90 degrees every 45 minutes to ensure an even char on all sides.

Once the internal temperature reaches about 160°F, it's time for the best part: the carve. Using a very sharp carving knife, slice thin, vertical strips from the outer edge of the meat. This "shaving" technique mimics the street taco experience. If some parts aren't charred enough, you can toss the sliced meat in a hot cast-iron skillet for 60 seconds to get those crispy, burnt edges.

Serving Your Home-Made Tacos

Serve your sliced pork on warm, small corn tortillas. The traditional way is to top each taco with a slice of the roasted pineapple from the top of the skewer, finely diced white onion, fresh cilantro, and a squeeze of lime. Don't forget a side of spicy salsa verde to cut through the richness of the pork.

The beauty of using a stainless steel vertical skewer pan for oven-roasted gyros is that it keeps the meat juicy. The vertical orientation prevents the pork from boiling in its own juices, which often happens in a slow cooker or a standard roasting pan.

FAQ

Can I use a different cut of meat?

Pork shoulder (butt) is the gold standard because the fat content keeps the meat moist during the long roasting process. While you can use pork loin, it is much leaner and prone to drying out when roasted vertically.

How do I stop the meat from sliding down the skewer?

The key is to pack the meat tightly. Use your palms to compress the layers as you build the stack. If you feel it slipping, you can use a few toothpicks to pin the layers together or ensure your skewer is pushed firmly into the base of the pan.

Do I really need the pineapple on top?

While you can omit it, the pineapple is functional, not just decorative. The enzymes in the pineapple help break down the proteins in the pork, and the dripping juice provides a natural glaze that is essential for an authentic al pastor taste.
