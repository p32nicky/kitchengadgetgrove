  • Check the starter’s rise: it should double in size within 4–6 hours after feeding.
  • Look for bubbles and a pleasant tangy smell; any pink or orange hue means discard.
  • Keep feeding schedule consistent—ideally every 12 hours if kept at room temperature, or weekly if refrigerated.
  • Use a clear glass jar to monitor activity without opening the lid.

When you’re ready to bake, give the starter a final feed 8–12 hours before you plan to mix the dough. This timing ensures peak activity and a strong rise.

2. Mixing the Dough – Tools & Techniques

Mixing a high‑hydration sourdough can be sticky and intimidating. Here’s where the sourdough baking checklist really shines:

  1. Measure flour and water by weight for accuracy.
  2. Combine ingredients in a large bowl; let the mixture autolyse for 30‑45 minutes. This rest allows the flour to absorb water, reducing mixing time.
  3. After autolyse, add the starter and salt. Use the Danish dough whisk from the 3‑in‑1 Sourdough Whisk Set to incorporate the starter fully without over‑working the dough.
  4. Perform a series of stretch‑and‑folds every 30 minutes during the bulk fermentation. The dough whisk helps lift the dough cleanly, and the dough scraper is perfect for scraping the bowl’s sides.

Each stretch‑and‑fold should last about 20 seconds per fold. By the end of bulk fermentation, the dough should be smooth, elastic, and slightly tacky.

3. Shaping & Proofing – Keep the Checklist Tight

Shaping is where many beginners lose confidence. Follow these checklist items for consistent results:

  • Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface and pre‑shape into a loose round.
  • Let it rest 15–20 minutes to relax gluten.
  • Use the dough scraper to create tension by pulling the dough toward you while rotating it.
  • Place the shaped loaf seam‑side up in a well‑floured proofing basket or bowl.
  • Cover and proof at 75°F (24°C) for 2–4 hours, or refrigerate overnight for a slower, flavor‑developing proof.

When proofing in the fridge, the dough will be ready to bake directly from the refrigerator—no need to warm up.

4. Scoring & Baking – The Final Checklist Steps

The moment of truth arrives when the loaf hits the oven. A crisp crust and open crumb start with a good score. The 3‑in‑1 Sourdough Whisk Set includes a bread scoring lame that gives you clean, confident cuts.

  1. Preheat your oven with a Dutch oven or baking stone inside to 475°F (245°C) for at least 30 minutes.
  2. Turn the proofed loaf onto a parchment square. Lightly dust the surface with flour.
  3. Using the lame, make swift, decisive cuts—about ¼‑inch deep—at a 45‑degree angle. The cuts control where the loaf expands.
  4. Transfer the loaf (with parchment) into the hot Dutch oven, cover, and bake 20 minutes.
  5. Remove the lid and bake another 20–25 minutes until the crust is deep golden brown and the internal temperature reaches 210°F (99°C).
  6. Cool on a rack for at least an hour before slicing; this lets the crumb set fully.

Every step in this sourdough baking checklist is designed to reduce guesswork and increase repeatable success.

5. Cleaning & Storing Your Tools

After a bake, the last thing you want is a greasy kitchen. The Danish dough whisk and scraper are stainless steel, so a quick rinse and a dab of dish soap remove dough residue. The lame’s blade should be wiped clean with a damp cloth and stored in its protective sheath to keep it sharp for the next loaf.

Keeping your tools dry and organized encourages you to stick to the checklist and bake more often.

FAQ

Can I use the sourdough whisk for non‑sourdough breads?

Absolutely. The whisk works well with any high‑hydration dough, from focaccia to ciabatta, because its flexible wires reduce splashing while still providing good incorporation.

How often should I replace the scoring lame?

The lame’s blade stays sharp for many loaves. If you notice it dragging or producing ragged cuts, a quick sharpening with a fine‑grit stone will revive it. Otherwise, replace it when the handle shows signs of wear.

Is the 3‑in‑1 set suitable for left‑handed bakers?

Yes. Both the whisk and scraper have symmetrical designs, and the lame’s handle is ambidextrous, making it comfortable for bakers of any dominant hand.
