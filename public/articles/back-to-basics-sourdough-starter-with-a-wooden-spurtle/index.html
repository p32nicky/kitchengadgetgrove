Why a Wooden Spurtle Matters in Sourdough Starter Basics

When you’re building and feeding a sourdough starter, the tools you use can influence both the texture of the dough and the longevity of your starter. A wooden spurtle—long, slender, and flexible—lets you mix without breaking the gluten structure. It also keeps the starter from sticking to the container, so you can scrape the sides clean without a knife or plastic spatula that might introduce unwanted microbes.

Getting Started: The First 5 Days of Your Starter

Follow these simple steps, and you’ll have a healthy, bubbly starter ready for baking. The wooden spurtle is your best friend during this period.

  1. Day 1 – Combine Flour and Water: Mix 100 g of all‑purpose flour with 100 ml of lukewarm water in a wide jar. Stir until there are no dry pockets. Use the wooden spurtle to fold the mixture gently, avoiding vigorous whisking that can aerate the dough too much.
  2. Day 2 – First Feeding: Discard half the mixture (about 100 g) and add 100 g of flour + 100 ml water. Mix again with the spurtle, letting the dough rise slowly in a warm spot.
  3. Day 3 – Second Feeding: Repeat the discard‑and‑feed routine. You should start seeing small bubbles. The wooden spurtle helps you observe the bubble distribution without disturbing the surface.
  4. Day 4 – Third Feeding: The starter should be doubling in volume within 6–8 hours. Use the spurtle to scrape the sides of the jar, ensuring no dried starter sticks and creates a clean environment for the wild yeast.
  5. Day 5 – Ready for Baking: If the starter doubles quickly and has a pleasant, tangy aroma, it’s ready for use. Keep feeding it daily if you’ll bake often, or store it in the fridge and feed it weekly.

Common Starter Problems and How the Spurtle Helps Fix Them

Even seasoned bakers face issues. Here’s how a wooden spurtle can help:

  • Starter Not Rising: Use the spurtle to stir the starter in a circular motion, encouraging even fermentation. If bubbles are sparse, try a warmer spot or a slightly larger flour‑to‑water ratio.
  • Dry or Stuck Starter: A quick scrape with the skinny spatula removes dried bits that can choke the yeast. This keeps the environment moist and active.
  • Over‑Aerated Dough: When mixing dough that already contains starter, a gentle whisking with the Danish dough whisk prevents over‑blooming of gluten, preserving the desired chew.

Storage Tips: Keeping Your Starter Fresh in the Fridge

Once your starter is established, store it in a glass jar with a loose lid. Every 7–10 days, remove the starter, discard half, and feed it again. A small wooden spurtle makes this process quick—just stir, scrape, and refill. The wood’s natural breathability also helps prevent mold growth, which can happen with plastic tools.

From Starter to Dough: Using the Spurtle for Bread Baking

  • Shape the loaf, proof, and bake. The wooden tools keep your hands clean and the dough free from metal‑induced dullness.

FAQ

Can I use the wooden spurtle on a non‑glass jar?

Yes. The spurtle’s wood is gentle on any container, but glass is preferred for visibility and heat resistance.

Do I need to sanitize the spurtle before first use?

Wash it with warm, soapy water and dry thoroughly. The natural oils in the wood will help keep it non‑porous.

How often should I replace my wooden spurtle set?

With proper care—washing and drying after each use—they can last many years. Replace only if you notice cracks or persistent odor.
