There is a specific kind of magic that happens in the first ten minutes of baking a loaf of sourdough. As the heat hits the dough, the bread expands rapidly—a phenomenon known as "oven spring." If you don't give that gas a place to escape, your bread will burst at its weakest point, often resulting in a messy, unpredictable tear on the bottom or side.

Learning artisanal bread scoring techniques is the difference between a rustic home loaf and a professional-looking boule. Why Scoring Matters for Your Crust

When dough ferments, carbon dioxide builds up inside the gluten network. In the oven, those gases expand. Without a strategic cut, the pressure builds until the crust ruptures randomly. By scoring, you are essentially creating a "relief valve."

A deep, confident cut allows the bread to expand upward rather than outward. This results in a taller loaf with a more open crumb. Beyond the physics, scoring allows you to express your creativity, turning a simple ball of dough into a canvas for wheat stalks, leaves, or geometric patterns.

Basic Scoring Patterns for Beginners

Before you attempt complex artistry, master these three foundational cuts. Regardless of the pattern, always hold your blade at a slight angle (about 30 to 45 degrees) rather than cutting straight down.

  • The Single Slash: A single long cut down the center of the loaf. This is ideal for batards (oval loaves) and creates a classic, rustic look.
  • The Cross (or X): Two intersecting cuts in the center of a boule. This encourages an even, symmetrical rise in all directions.
  • The Box Score: A square cut in the center with smaller decorative slashes in the corners. This is a great way to practice precision and control.

Advanced Tips for a Professional Finish

Once you are comfortable with the basics, you can start experimenting with "ear" creation. The "ear" is that raised, crispy ridge of crust that curls back from the cut. To achieve this, you must score at a very shallow angle—almost parallel to the dough—rather than slicing vertically.

Another pro tip is to chill your dough. Scoring a room-temperature loaf can be like trying to cut soft butter; the dough sticks to the blade. Cold dough, straight from the refrigerator, is firmer and much easier to score with precision. Common Scoring Mistakes to Avoid

The most common mistake beginners make is cutting too shallowly. A superficial scratch won't provide enough relief for the oven spring, leading to side-bursts. Don't be afraid to go about half an inch deep into the dough.

Conversely, avoid "hesitation marks." If you pause mid-cut, you'll leave a small notch that can cause the bread to tear unevenly. The key is a fast, fluid motion. Think of it as one quick swipe rather than a slow carve. If you find the blade is dragging, it's time to swap it for a fresh razor.

FAQ

What is the best angle for scoring sourdough?

For a standard expansion, a 45-degree angle is best. If you want to create a prominent "ear," decrease that angle to about 30 degrees, slicing more across the surface than into it.

Can I score my bread after it goes in the oven?

No. Scoring must be done immediately before the loaf is placed in the oven. Once the heat hits the dough and the crust begins to set, you can no longer control where the bread expands.

How often should I change the blade in my lame?

You should use a fresh blade for every single loaf. Even a single use can dull the edge enough to cause dragging, which ruins the clean lines of your artisanal bread scoring techniques.
