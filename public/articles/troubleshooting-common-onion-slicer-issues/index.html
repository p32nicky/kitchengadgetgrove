  • Clean the blade. Food residue builds up quickly. Use a soft brush and warm, soapy water, then dry thoroughly.

When the blade still misbehaves, consider swapping it out. Most slicers use standard stainless‑steel blades that are inexpensive to replace.

Cleaning and Maintenance: Extending the Life of Your Tool

Proper upkeep is often overlooked, but a clean slicer performs better and lasts longer.

After each use:

  1. Disassemble the slicer if possible. Separate the blade, handle, and any adjustable parts.
  2. Rinse under warm water, using a soft brush to remove stuck particles.
  3. Dry completely with a lint‑free cloth. Moisture is the enemy of stainless steel.
  4. Apply a light coat of food‑safe oil to the blade’s surface to prevent rust.

The stainless‑steel prongs of the Onion Holder & Slicing Guide are also dishwasher safe, but a quick hand‑wash will keep them looking like new.

When to Replace vs. Repair

Not every problem warrants a brand‑new slicer. Use this guideline:

  • Blade dullness. If sharpening doesn’t restore a clean cut, replace the blade.
  • Cracked or warped frame. Structural damage can’t be fixed safely; replace the unit.
  • Loose hinges or screws. Tighten or replace the hardware. Often a simple screwdriver fix restores function.
