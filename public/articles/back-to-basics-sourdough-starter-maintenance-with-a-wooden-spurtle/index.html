Why a Wooden Spurtle Beats Plastic or Metal

Wood is naturally porous and gentle on dough. It doesn’t leach flavors or cling to the starter, unlike metal. A wooden spurtle’s thin edge gets into every nook of a jar, ensuring even hydration and preventing dry pockets that can lead to mold. Plus, wood is a non‑scratch surface—great for preserving the integrity of your glass or ceramic container.

Daily Starter Routine: Feed, Mix, Scrape

  1. Measure the Feed: For a 100‑gram starter, give it 50 grams of flour and 50 grams of water. A 1:1 ratio keeps the microbes balanced.
  2. Scrape the Jar: Use the skinny wooden spurtle to scrape the sides and bottom. This distributes the starter’s natural yeast throughout the mixture.
  3. Mix Thoroughly: Swirl the mixture with the spurtle until it’s uniform. A smooth consistency helps the starter rise evenly.
  4. Cover and Store: Seal the jar with a breathable lid or cloth. Keep it at room temperature (70–75°F) and feed it twice a day for the first week, then every other day once it stabilizes.

With a wooden spurtle, each step feels deliberate and satisfying—no splashing, no mess. The set’s dual pieces offer versatility: one is a classic spurtle for mixing, the other a skinny Danish‑style dough stick for reaching into tight corners.

Spot‑Cleaning and Jar Maintenance

Over time, starter can leave stubborn residue on jar walls. A wooden spurtle’s flexible edge is perfect for gentle scraping. Avoid using a metal spoon, which can scratch glass and create micro‑cracks where mold can hide. After scraping, rinse the jar with warm water and let it air dry before the next feeding.

Seasonal Adjustments: Temperature and Hydration

When the weather swings, so does the starter’s activity. In colder months, you might need to increase the feeding frequency or use a slightly thicker starter (e.g., 60% flour, 40% water). The wooden spurtle’s thin blade makes it easy to adjust the hydration level by adding a few drops of water or flour while mixing, ensuring you never over‑ or under‑hydrate.

Storage Tips for Long‑Term Hobbyists

If you’re going on a trip or need a break, you can refrigerate the starter. Use the spurtle to mix it before sealing the jar and refrigerate at 35–40°F. When you’re ready to revive it, let it sit at room temperature for 12–24 hours, then feed and mix with the wooden spurtle to reactivate the yeasts.

FAQ

Can I use the wooden spurtle on a stainless‑steel mixer?

Yes, but keep in mind wood can scratch metal if used aggressively. Use gentle strokes and avoid pressing too hard.

How often should I replace my wooden spurtle?

Wooden tools last a long time when cared for properly. Rinse after each use, dry thoroughly, and store in a dry place. Replace only if you notice cracks or warping.

Do I need to sanitize the spurtle between feedings?

No, the natural oils in the wood and the acidic environment of sourdough keep microbes in check. Just rinse and dry after each use.
