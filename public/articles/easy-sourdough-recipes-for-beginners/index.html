Recipe #1: Classic No‑Knead Sourdough

This loaf uses a high‑hydration dough that requires only a couple of stretch‑and‑folds instead of traditional kneading.

  1. Ingredients (makes one 2‑lb loaf):
    • 500 g bread flour (about 4 cups)
    • 380 g water, lukewarm (1 ½ cups)
    • 100 g active sourdough starter (½ cup)
    • 10 g salt (1 ½ tsp)
  2. Mix: In a large bowl, combine flour and water. Use the Danish dough whisk from the Sourdough Whisk Set to stir until no dry spots remain. Cover and let rest 30 minutes (autolyse).
  3. Add starter & salt: Add the starter and salt, then whisk again until fully incorporated. The dough will look shaggy.
  4. Bulk fermentation: Let the dough sit at room temperature for 4 hours, performing a set of stretch‑and‑folds every 30 minutes (4 folds total). Use the scraper to lift and fold the dough over itself – this builds gluten without heavy kneading.
  5. Shape & proof: Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface, shape into a tight round, and place seam‑side up in a well‑floured proofing basket. Cover and refrigerate overnight (12‑14 hours).
  6. Bake: Preheat a Dutch oven to 475 °F. Transfer the dough, score with the lame, cover, and bake 20 minutes. Remove the lid and bake another 25 minutes until deep brown.

Result: A crusty, airy loaf with a chewy crumb – perfect proof that simple sourdough recipes can deliver bakery‑level results.

Recipe #2: Quick 24‑Hour Sourdough Boule

If you’d rather see results sooner, this 24‑hour schedule trims the bulk time while still giving you a flavorful loaf.

  • Ingredients (one boule): 450 g all‑purpose flour, 330 g water, 90 g starter, 9 g salt.
  • Day 1 – Morning: Mix flour, water, and starter with the whisk. Autolyse 20 minutes.
  • Day 1 – Midday: Add salt, whisk again, then perform three stretch‑and‑folds spaced 20 minutes apart.
  • Day 1 – Evening: Let the dough rest, covered, for 3 hours. Shape, place in a floured banneton, and refrigerate overnight.
  • Day 2 – Morning: Preheat oven and Dutch oven, score with the lame, and bake as in the first recipe.

This method shows you that you don’t need a week‑long ferment to achieve a tangy crumb. The shorter schedule still allows enough acid development for that signature sourdough flavor.

Tips for Consistent Results

Even with simple recipes, a few habits make a big difference:

  • Maintain a healthy starter: Feed it at least once a day if kept at room temperature, or weekly if refrigerated. A bubbly, aromatic starter is the foundation of every loaf.
  • Control temperature: Aim for a dough temperature of 75 °F. If your kitchen is hotter, shorten bulk fermentation; if cooler, extend it.
  • Use the right flour: Bread flour provides more gluten, which helps the dough hold its shape. You can swap 20‑30% of the flour for whole‑wheat for added flavor, but keep the hydration the same.
  • Score confidently: The lame in the Sourdough Whisk Set lets you make clean, shallow cuts. A good score releases steam and guides the loaf’s expansion.
  • Practice steam: Baking in a covered Dutch oven creates a steamy environment that yields a glossy crust. If you don’t have a Dutch oven, place a pan of boiling water on the oven floor.

FAQ

Do I need a Dutch oven to bake sourdough?

A Dutch oven isn’t mandatory, but it creates a sealed, steamy environment that mimics professional steam ovens. If you don’t have one, use a baking stone with a pan of hot water on the lower rack, and cover the loaf with a large metal bowl for the first 20 minutes.

Can I use the 3‑in‑1 Sourdough Whisk Set with whole‑grain flours?

Absolutely. The whisk handles heavier whole‑grain doughs just as well as white flour. You may need to add a bit more water because whole‑grain flours absorb more, but the scraper will help you adjust the dough’s consistency during the bulk stage.

How often should I refresh my starter if I bake once a week?

Feed the starter at least once a week if you store it in the fridge. For weekly baking, take it out a day before, feed it twice (12‑hour intervals), and let it become active and bubbly before using it in your recipe.
